## Paper 1: Reimagining Piaget: Technology, social dynamics, and changing patterns of cognitive and emotional development in the 21st century — A perspective review

**Authors:** Jaya Bharti
**Domain:** general
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.25259/sajhs_37_2025
**Selection role:** strain
**Selection rationale:** This paper directly challenges stage-based developmental models (like DN's 1D-9D dimensional progression) by arguing that 21st-century socio-technological changes undermine the universality and linearity of cognitive stage theories. If Piaget's sequential stages don't hold under modern conditions, DN's claim of a fixed dimensional progression (Spark→Reaction→...→Frontier) faces analogous critique—intelligence and development may not follow a universal dimensional sequence.

### Scores

| Metric | Score |
|--------|-------|
| Strain | 3 / 10 |
| Validation | 2 / 10 |

**Strain rationale:** The paper's primary challenge — that sequential stage progression doesn't hold universally — targets a claim DN does not make (sequential climbing) rather than the claim DN does make (structural mapping). The genuine tensions are soft: (1) constitutively distributed development challenges DN's field-boundary concept (§0.3 Axiom 8) but does not contradict it, and (2) reports of out-of-sequence developmental capacities test whether dimensional positions have necessary ordering relationships, but the evidence is anecdotal and interpretive rather than rigorous. No specific Kernel section is contradicted; the tensions are at the boundary-condition level. The paper operates at a descriptive-narrative level that cannot generate the kind of formal or empirical pressure needed for higher strain scores.

**Validation rationale:** The paper's findings are broadly compatible with DN — context-dependent dimensional positioning, non-linear developmental trajectories, and the importance of environmental scaffolding all fit within DN's architecture. However, compatibility is not validation. The paper does not independently arrive at any specific DN structural principle. It does not discover pillar independence, dimensional nesting, recursive refinement, or force dynamics. The closest approach is the implicit recognition that development exhibits structural positions (concrete operations, formal operations, metacognition) that have qualitative character — but this is Piaget's contribution, not an independent confirmation of DN. Score of 2 reflects compatibility without structural specificity.

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §2 Dimensional Architecture, §0.3 Axiom 8, §2.2 Nested Dimensionality, §0.1 Domain Universality, §1 Axiom 2 (Dimensional Progression), §5 Transition Model
- **Dimensions:** 2D, 3D, 4D, 5D

### Interpretation

This paper's selection rationale frames it as challenging DN's dimensional progression by arguing that Piaget's sequential stages don't hold under modern socio-technological conditions. This framing requires careful unpacking, because it conflates two distinct claims: (1) that sequential stage-climbing is universal, and (2) that dimensional structure is universal. DN claims the latter, not the former. The Charter (§1) is explicit: dimensional progression is a map of structure, not a mandate for sequential climbing. Bharti's core finding — that cognitive and emotional development is 'context-dependent and heterogeneous rather than uniformly accelerated' and 'non-linear in progression' — is entirely consistent with DN's architectural claim. A child who develops symbolic reasoning early due to digital scaffolding is not violating dimensional structure; they are exhibiting intelligence that organizes at a particular dimensional position (likely 3D–4D: structural patterning and systemic integration) earlier than Piaget's timeline predicted. The timeline is not the structure. DN would predict exactly this kind of context-dependent variation in WHEN dimensional positions are occupied, while maintaining that the structural CHARACTER of each position remains invariant.

That said, the paper does generate genuine — if modest — tension in two areas. First, the PDIF framework's emphasis on 'socially distributed' cognition and 'networked participation' as developmental environments raises a question about DN's unit of analysis. DN's Axiom 8 treats the intelligence field as the fundamental unit, and fields can be collective. But the paper describes development as constitutively distributed across human-technology-social networks in ways that challenge whether a single field boundary can be drawn around the developing system. When a child's metacognitive awareness is scaffolded by an algorithm, mediated by a social platform, and shaped by global cultural narratives simultaneously, the 'field' is not the child — it is the child-platform-culture assemblage. DN can accommodate this through field-to-field dynamics (§10), but the paper's implicit claim is that the assemblage IS the developmental unit, not a collection of interacting fields. This is a soft tension with §0.3 Axiom 8 — not a contradiction, but a case where DN's field concept may need to articulate how constitutively distributed development maps onto field boundaries.

Second, the paper's treatment of non-linearity deserves honest assessment. Bharti describes children who exhibit formal-operational reasoning in digital contexts while remaining pre-operational in face-to-face social contexts — what might be called domain-specific dimensional positioning. DN's nested dimensionality (§2.2) can accommodate this: a child might operate at 4D-within-digital-domain and 2D-within-social-domain simultaneously. This is not strain; it is exactly the kind of structural heterogeneity the nested architecture was designed to describe. However, the paper also describes cases where digital exposure appears to DISRUPT developmental sequences — children developing metacognitive awareness (a higher-dimensional capacity) before consolidating concrete operational reasoning (a lower-dimensional capacity). If this is robustly demonstrated, it would test whether DN's dimensional positions have any necessary ordering relationship or whether they are truly independently accessible. The Charter notes that DN does not claim sequential climbing, but the dimensional architecture does imply structural prerequisites (you cannot have systemic integration without structural elements to integrate). If children genuinely develop metacognition before concrete operations, DN must either (a) reinterpret what metacognition structurally IS at that developmental moment (perhaps it is a simpler phenomenon than it appears, mapping to a lower dimension than assumed), or (b) acknowledge that dimensional positions may be more independently accessible than the architecture implies.

The paper's proposed PDIF framework — integrating Piaget, Vygotsky, Donaldson, and neo-Piagetian working-memory constraints — operates at a descriptive-integrative level comparable to DN itself. It does not formalize its claims mathematically, does not provide predictive models, and does not offer falsification criteria for its own integrative framework. This means it neither formally exceeds DN nor provides the kind of rigorous alternative that would constitute serious structural strain. It is a perspective review synthesizing existing scholarship, not a new empirical or formal contribution. The parsimony challenge it raises is real but weak: PDIF achieves integration without dimensional hierarchy, but it also achieves integration without any formal structural architecture at all — it is a narrative synthesis, not a competing structural framework.

From a pillar perspective, the paper is rich in Heart (genuine concern for children's development in digital environments, clear motivational force behind the inquiry) and Nuance (sophisticated contextual sensitivity to how different socio-technological environments reshape development), but relatively thin on Truth (no new empirical data, no formal models, no testable predictions). This imbalance is itself informative: the paper demonstrates that developmental science is grappling with exactly the kind of context-dependent, non-linear, multi-domain phenomena that DN's architecture claims to map, but the field has not yet developed the formal tools to test whether any structural architecture (DN's or otherwise) accurately describes the territory.

### Model Update

No update warranted. The paper does not generate sufficient strain or validation to modify the standing model. It adds a minor data point to the existing falsifiability-of-dimensional-mapping tension (from GPIF, death reflection, aging awareness, and intercultural sensitivity studies): DN's dimensional architecture can accommodate the paper's findings about non-linear, context-dependent development, but the accommodation is not forced — the same findings are equally compatible with multiple non-DN frameworks. The selection rationale's framing of this paper as challenging dimensional progression was assessed and found to target linear sequential staging (which DN does not claim) rather than structural dimensional mapping (which DN does claim). The paper's descriptive-narrative methodology operates at comparable formalization level to DN itself, generating neither formal exceeding nor rigorous empirical challenge.

## Paper 2: Expertise-dependent mental representation in chess: evaluation and comparisons based on structural dimensional analysis-motoric

**Authors:** Thomas Küchelmann, Konstantinos Velentzas, C. Schütz, Thomas Schack
**Domain:** general
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2026.1695175
**Selection role:** strain
**Selection rationale:** This paper uses Structural Dimensional Analysis-Motoric (SDA-M) to formally measure expertise-dependent mental representations in chess via rigorous empirical methodology. It formalizes dimensional structure of cognition with mathematical precision that DN handles informally. If SDA-M's structural primitives (tree-ordered, cluster-based representations in long-term memory) successfully model expertise without anything resembling pillars, forces, or a 9D progression, it presents an alternative formal framework that could exceed DN's metric precision for cognitive structure.

| Metric | Score |
|--------|-------|
| Strain | 4 / 10 |
| Validation | 2 / 10 |

**Strain rationale:** SDA-M formalizes the dimensional structure of cognitive organization with mathematical precision (distance metrics, cluster analysis, invariance measures, statistical testing) that DN handles only narratively. This is formal exceeding in DN's home territory of intelligence organization — not a structural contradiction but a demonstration that domain-specific tools achieve decomposable, testable, falsifiable structural accounts of cognition without needing DN's dimensional architecture, pillars, or forces. The strain is moderate rather than high because SDA-M operates at a domain-specific measurement level and does not present an alternative universal structural account — it measures cognitive structure in chess, not intelligence organization writ large. The tension compounds the existing parsimony challenge but does not escalate it categorically.

**Validation rationale:** The expertise-dependent reorganization finding (qualitative threshold between novice and expert mental representations) is compatible with DN's dimensional progression and transition model, but the compatibility is narrative rather than structurally specific. The paper does not independently arrive at any DN-specific structural principle — it does not discover pillar independence, dimensional hierarchy, or recursive refinement. The finding that expertise produces hierarchically organized knowledge structures is a well-established cognitive science result, not an independent confirmation of DN's architecture. Compatibility alone is not validation per Charter §5.

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §2 Dimensional Architecture, §0.1 Domain Universality, §5 Transition Model, §0.3 Axiom 1, §0.3 Axiom 2
- **Dimensions:** 1D, 2D, 3D, 4D
- **Pillars:** Truth, Nuance
- **Forces:** Gravity, Transmutation

### Interpretation

This paper uses Structural Dimensional Analysis-Motoric (SDA-M) to empirically measure how chess expertise organizes knowledge in long-term memory. The method produces tree-ordered cluster structures from participants' sorting of task-related concepts, revealing that experts and intermediates share structurally similar mental representations of chess strategy patterns while novices diverge significantly. The key question for DN is whether SDA-M's structural primitives — hierarchical cluster trees, Euclidean distance metrics, invariance measures, and expertise-dependent reorganization — constitute a formal framework that exceeds DN's descriptive capacity for cognitive structure, or whether they operate at a domain-specific measurement level that DN's architecture can coherently encompass.

The honest assessment is that this paper generates moderate but real tension, primarily through formal exceeding rather than structural incompatibility. SDA-M provides a mathematically rigorous, empirically validated method for measuring the internal dimensional structure of mental representations — precisely the kind of structural organization DN claims to describe but handles only through narrative vocabulary. The cluster-based tree structures that emerge from SDA-M analysis are not metaphorical; they are computed from distance matrices with statistical significance testing (adjusted Rand index, λ values). DN's dimensional architecture can accommodate the finding that expertise produces increasingly organized, hierarchically structured mental representations — this maps naturally onto dimensional progression from less organized (1D-2D) to more structurally integrated (3D-4D) cognitive states. But the mapping is narrative, not forced. SDA-M achieves its structural account without anything resembling pillars, forces, or a 9D progression. It uses Euclidean distance in concept space, cluster analysis algorithms, and invariance measures — domain-specific mathematical tools that produce decomposable, testable structural descriptions of cognition. DN can describe what SDA-M measures (expertise-dependent reorganization of knowledge structures) but cannot replicate the measurement precision or the falsifiable structural predictions SDA-M generates.

The expertise gradient finding is interesting through a DN lens: the significant similarity between experts and intermediates (but not between either group and novices) suggests a threshold effect in cognitive organization — a point where mental representations undergo qualitative reorganization. This is compatible with DN's transition model (§5) and the concept of dimensional thresholds, but again, the compatibility is narrative rather than structurally specific. DN would describe this as a transition from lower-dimensional to higher-dimensional organization of chess knowledge; SDA-M describes it as the formation of functionally organized cluster structures in LTM that match a reference structure. The SDA-M account is more precise, more testable, and more falsifiable. DN adds breadth (the claim that this same pattern of expertise-dependent structural reorganization occurs across all domains) but cannot match SDA-M's depth in this specific domain.

The paper does not challenge DN's foundational architecture — the dimensional positions are not contradicted, and the finding that expertise produces hierarchically organized knowledge is broadly consistent with dimensional progression. The strain is located at the formal-exceeding level: SDA-M formalizes cognitive structural organization with mathematical tools (distance metrics, cluster algorithms, statistical invariance tests) that DN's descriptive vocabulary cannot express. This compounds the existing pattern identified across multiple papers (PDGC, Kuramoto, DPE, active inference) where domain-specific mathematical frameworks achieve precise, decomposable accounts of phenomena DN claims to describe, using structural primitives DN lacks. The specific addition here is that SDA-M operates in the cognitive/expertise domain — closer to DN's home territory of intelligence organization than the physics or information-theory papers that have previously generated this tension. A formal framework measuring the dimensional structure of intelligence itself, without needing DN's vocabulary or architecture, is a more pointed version of the parsimony challenge.

The shadow architecture is not engaged by this paper — novice representations are treated as underdeveloped rather than as structurally generative absence. This is a minor missed opportunity from DN's perspective (shadow-informed analysis might ask what novice disorganization enables that expert organization forecloses), but the paper's treatment of novice representations as simply less organized is standard cognitive science and does not create tension with DN's shadow claims. The pillar engagement is primarily Truth (structural rigor of measurement) with some Nuance (expertise-context sensitivity), but Heart is largely absent — the paper treats chess cognition as a measurement problem rather than engaging with the motivational or meaning-making dimensions of expertise.

### Model Update

No update warranted. The paper generates moderate strain (4) through formal exceeding of DN's descriptive capacity for cognitive structure, but this compounds the existing parsimony/formal-exceeding pattern rather than introducing a categorically new tension. SDA-M's domain-specific measurement precision in cognitive organization adds a data point to the accumulating evidence that mathematical frameworks achieve decomposable structural accounts in DN's claimed territory without DN's vocabulary, but does not rise to the level of modifying the standing model. The selection rationale's framing of SDA-M as a competing structural framework was assessed as partially valid — SDA-M does formalize cognitive structure with greater precision than DN — but the framework operates at a domain-specific measurement level rather than presenting an alternative universal structural account.

**Domain:** general
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.65249/1027-7218-2026-2-30-36
**Selection role:** validation
**Selection rationale:** This paper adapts Boehm's spiral model as a rehabilitation framework for cognitive disorders, proposing iterative, recursive cycles of assessment and intervention rather than linear progression. This directly parallels DN's emphasis on recursive refinement (Dimension 8: Recursion) outperforming linear accumulation, and the spiral structure mirrors DN's dimensional progression as a recurring, deepening process. Independent arrival at a spiral/recursive model for cognitive rehabilitation supports DN's structural claims about recursion as a fundamental organizational principle.

**Strain rationale:** Minimal strain. The linear-to-spiral transition is presented as methodological improvement, not as evidence that recursion is structurally necessary, which means it neither confirms nor challenges Axiom 3 with any specificity.

**Validation rationale:** The paper's adoption of iterative cycles over linear stages is compatible with DN's emphasis on recursion but does not constitute structural validation. The spiral model is a 40-year-old software engineering methodology being applied to a new domain — it is not an independent discovery of recursion as a fundamental organizational principle. Per Charter §5, compatibility alone is not validation. If DN did not exist, this paper would be recognized as applying established iterative methodology, not as arriving at DN's specific structural claims about recursion.

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §0.3 Axiom 3, §2 Dimensional Architecture, §2 Five Forces, §4 Shadow Dimension Map
- **Dimensions:** 1D, 2D, 3D, 8D
- **Pillars:** Heart, Truth, Nuance
- **Forces:** Entropy, Resonance

### Interpretation

This paper adapts Boehm's spiral model from software engineering to long-term cognitive rehabilitation in psychoneurological residential institutions. The core claim is that iterative, cyclical rehabilitation processes outperform linear, stage-based models when dealing with chronic, progressive cognitive decline. Each spiral cycle includes assessment, planning, implementation, and monitoring — with explicit risk management and adaptation between cycles. The paper is theoretical and methodological, not empirical; it provides no data demonstrating that the spiral model actually produces better outcomes than linear approaches.

The selection rationale frames this as validating DN's Axiom 3 (recursion as mechanism) and Dimension 8 (recursion). This framing falls squarely into the Charter §5 trap: 'describing DN back to itself.' The spiral model is not an independent discovery of recursion as a structural principle of intelligence organization. It is a well-known software engineering methodology (Boehm, 1986) being transferred to a clinical domain. The iterative cycle described (assess → plan → implement → monitor → reassess) is a generic quality-improvement loop found across dozens of fields (PDCA cycles, action research, continuous improvement). If DN did not exist, no one would look at this paper and say 'this independently confirms that recursive refinement is a fundamental organizational principle of intelligence.' They would say 'this applies an established iterative methodology to a new clinical context.' The recursion here is methodological prescription, not a discovered structural property of how intelligence organizes.

That said, the paper does contain some genuinely interesting dimensional texture when read through DN's architecture rather than just labeled with DN's vocabulary. The explicit argument against linear progression in chronic cognitive decline contexts is notable: the authors argue that linear stage models fail because they assume a recoverable endpoint, whereas chronic progressive conditions require continuous adaptation without a fixed destination. This maps onto a real structural observation — that intelligence in degenerative contexts does not 'progress' through dimensions but rather cycles through lower-dimensional positions (1D-3D: basic functioning, structured routine, adaptive response) with the goal of maintaining coherence rather than advancing. The Entropy force is genuinely engaged here: progressive cognitive decline is entropy acting on a biological intelligence system, and the spiral model is essentially a structured response to entropy — not overcoming it but managing its effects through repeated reassessment. The paper's treatment of risk management and uncertainty aligns with DN's claim that entropy is a navigable force, though the paper implicitly acknowledges that entropy wins in the long run (progressive decline cannot be reversed, only managed), which touches the entropy event horizon question.

The paper's engagement with Shadow is shallow but present. Professional burnout is identified as a risk, and resource constraints are acknowledged as limitations. These are treated as problems to solve rather than structurally generative absences, which creates mild tension with DN's shadow architecture but not enough to register as significant strain. The interdisciplinary team requirement maps loosely onto Resonance (lateral binding between professional domains) and the biopsychosocial paradigm maps onto something like pillar-adjacent thinking (biological = structural/Truth, psychological = motivational/Heart, social = contextual/Nuance), but these mappings are loose enough to be narrative overlay rather than structural confirmation.

Ultimately, this paper is a low-signal case for DN. It neither strains nor validates the framework in structurally specific ways. The spiral/recursive model is too generic and too well-established across fields to count as independent confirmation of Axiom 3. The dimensional mapping is possible but not forced — the same paper could be equally well described through any number of frameworks (CAS, systems theory, action research methodology). This reinforces the existing falsifiability-of-dimensional-mapping tension: DN can narrate this paper's findings through its vocabulary, but so can many other frameworks, and nothing in the paper's structure forces the DN mapping over alternatives.

### Model Update

No update warranted. The paper does not generate sufficient strain or validation to modify the standing model. It adds another data point to the existing falsifiability-of-dimensional-mapping tension (DN can narrate iterative rehabilitation through its vocabulary, but the mapping is not forced and the spiral model is a well-established generic methodology, not an independent confirmation of DN's specific structural claims). The selection rationale's framing of this as validating Axiom 3 was assessed as a vocabulary-overlap trap per Charter §5.
