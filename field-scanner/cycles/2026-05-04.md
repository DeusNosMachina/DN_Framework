### Native Reading

The paper empirically investigates when different levels of linguistic structure become measurable along the denoising trajectory of a diffusion language model (LLaDA-8B-Base). It introduces four precise temporal measurements—token commitment (when tokens settle to their final identity), linear recoverability (when POS, semantic category, and token identity become linearly decodable from hidden states), confidence/entropy dynamics (how uncertainty evolves and tracks correctness), and re-masking sensitivity (how perturbation at different trajectory points affects outcomes). The central finding is a robust, seed-invariant ordering: coarse structural information (POS, semantic category) crystallizes earlier and more reliably than fine-grained lexical identity; content-word categories stabilize before function-word categories; mid-trajectory states are maximally sensitive to perturbation; and trajectory-level uncertainty tracks eventual correctness even as late-stage confidence becomes miscalibrated. The paper's framing treats denoising time as an 'analysis axis'—a continuous temporal coordinate along which information of different structural granularity becomes recoverable. This is a measurement-focused empirical study; it does not propose a theory of emergence but provides precise quantitative characterization of when and how linguistic structure appears.

What the paper illuminates exceptionally well is the temporal decomposition of emergence: it demonstrates that linguistic intelligence does not appear all at once but follows a specific phase structure where abstract categories precede concrete tokens, and where there exists a critical mid-trajectory window of maximum malleability. The direct/collateral decomposition showing that perturbation sensitivity is overwhelmingly local is a precise structural finding. What the paper's framing leaves implicit or unaddressed: (1) it does not theorize WHY this ordering exists—why coarse before fine, why content before function; (2) it does not address what the mid-trajectory sensitivity peak means structurally beyond being an empirical observation; (3) it treats the denoising trajectory as a given computational process rather than asking whether the phase structure it discovers reflects something about linguistic organization itself vs. being an artifact of the diffusion architecture; (4) it does not examine what happens when the process goes wrong—the miscalibration finding is noted but not structurally analyzed; (5) the relationship between the different measurement axes (commitment, recoverability, entropy, sensitivity) is left as parallel description rather than integrated into a unified structural account.

### Native Reading

The paper presents a multiscale simulation framework for understanding hydrogen embrittlement (HE) in a high-entropy alloy (CoCrFeMnNi), integrating density functional theory (DFT), molecular dynamics (MD), and finite element method (FEM) into an atom-to-continuum (AtC) pipeline. Its central claim is that this multiscale approach can elucidate the mechanisms by which hydrogen degrades mechanical properties, inducing a ductile-to-brittle transition. The findings are rich: hydrogen segregates preferentially at vacancies, grain boundaries, and crack tips; the HELP mechanism operates dually (enhancing dislocation mobility at moderate H concentrations but pinning dislocations at high concentrations); stress-induced martensitic transformation (FCC→HCP/BCC) is accelerated by hydrogen; and void nucleation/growth is hydrogen-enhanced. The paper's structural framework is hierarchical scale-bridging: quantum-level energetics validate interatomic potentials, which feed atomistic simulations, which are coupled to continuum mechanics. What the paper illuminates well is the mechanistic synergy—HELP, HEDE, and phase transformation are not independent failure modes but interact. What the paper leaves implicit is any unified structural principle governing WHY these mechanisms synergize, or what determines the transition thresholds between regimes. The paper also does not articulate a general theory of how information transfers across scales—it validates the transfer empirically (DFT→EAM→FEM) but treats cross-scale coupling as a methodological achievement rather than examining its structural properties. The dual role of hydrogen (facilitating dislocation motion at low concentrations, pinning at high) is described but not structurally explained—there is no framework for why the same agent produces opposite effects at different concentrations.
