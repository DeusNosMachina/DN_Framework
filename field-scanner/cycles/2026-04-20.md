### Native Reading

Serrahima's paper is a work in analytic philosophy of mind that develops a methodological framework for studying self-consciousness by decomposing it into two domains: psychological self-consciousness (ψ-ownership) and bodily self-consciousness (φ-ownership). Building on Bermúdez's terminology, the paper establishes three desiderata that any adequate account of self-consciousness in either domain must satisfy: (1) it must explain the relevant form of immunity to error through misidentification (IEM), (2) it must account for the phenomenology of ownership in that domain, and (3) it must explain the relationship between IEM and phenomenology. The paper's central structural move is to show that these three desiderata apply 'mutatis mutandis' to both ψ and φ domains, thereby disclosing deep structural parallels between psychological and bodily self-consciousness that motivate an integrative account. The critical contribution is showing that Bermúdez's own approach, despite explicitly advocating integration, fails at a specific juncture: his explanation of bodily IEM in terms of spatial content undermines the phenomenology of φ-ownership he elsewhere defends, because it makes bodily self-ascription epistemically grounded in spatial representation rather than in ownership phenomenology, thereby breaking the ψ-φ structural parallel.

What the paper's own framing illuminates is the precise logical structure of the integrative problem: the three desiderata function as structural constraints that any theory must simultaneously satisfy, and the paper shows that satisfying them in the φ domain while maintaining structural parity with the ψ domain is harder than it appears. The ownership-based decomposition (ψ vs. φ) is elegant because it identifies a single structural primitive—ownership—that operates across both domains but with different phenomenological content. What the framing leaves implicit or unaddressed: (a) the paper does not theorize what drives the orientation toward integration in the first place—why should ψ and φ be unified rather than treated as independent? The integrative impulse is assumed rather than grounded. (b) The paper treats the ψ-φ distinction as exhaustive of self-consciousness, leaving no structural room for forms of self-consciousness that are neither psychological nor bodily (e.g., social, temporal, narrative). (c) The relationship between the three desiderata is presented as parallel constraints but their interdependence is not formalized—could they conflict? Under what conditions? (d) The paper operates entirely at the level of structural analysis of concepts and does not engage with developmental, dynamic, or processual dimensions of self-consciousness.

### Native Reading

The paper reviews the emerging field of neural reconstruction—translating brain activity into images, text, or speech via neuroimaging and deep learning—and examines its potential application to patients with disorders of consciousness (DoCs). Its structural framework is organized around three axes: (1) the technical pipeline (signal acquisition → decoding model → reconstructed output), (2) the clinical spectrum of consciousness (vegetative state / unresponsive wakefulness syndrome → minimally conscious state → covert consciousness), and (3) the ethical-interpretive challenges of applying reconstruction to patients who cannot validate outputs. The paper's central insight is that behavioral assessment systematically underestimates cognitive capacity in DoCs patients—neuroimaging reveals that a subset of patients classified as vegetative retain command-following, mental imagery, or other markers of awareness. Reconstruction could bridge this gap by providing a window into otherwise inaccessible mental content.

What the paper illuminates well: the mismatch between behavioral output and internal cognitive state, the technical requirements for reconstruction (training data, model validation, signal quality), and the ethical risks of misattribution (both false positives—attributing rich experience where none exists—and false negatives—missing genuine awareness). It offers a responsible framework of recommendations centered on awareness detection, model validation, and patient autonomy. What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit: (1) It treats 'consciousness' as a monolithic target variable that is either present or absent (or graded along a single axis of 'more/less aware'), without decomposing what structural dimensions of consciousness reconstruction might access versus which it cannot. (2) The paper conflates several structurally distinct problems under 'reconstruction'—detecting awareness, recovering experiential content, restoring communication, and preserving autonomy—without a framework for understanding why these are structurally independent challenges that may require different approaches. (3) The ethical analysis, while thoughtful, lacks a structural account of why misattribution is dangerous beyond 'it could harm the patient'—there is no framework for understanding the relationship between the reconstructed signal and the patient's orientational relationship to their own experience. (4) The spectrum from vegetative to minimally conscious to covert consciousness is treated as a continuum of 'more awareness' rather than as potentially involving qualitatively different organizational modes of consciousness.
