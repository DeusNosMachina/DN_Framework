### Native Reading

HFGT presents a rigorous mathematical framework for modeling systems-of-systems (SoS) by bridging Model-Based Systems Engineering (MBSE) and network science. Its core innovation is preserving the heterogeneity of engineering system descriptions—form, function, and concept—within graph-theoretic structures that remain mathematically actionable. The framework is built on linguistic primitives: resources as subjects, system processes as predicates, and operands (matter, energy, organisms, information, money) as objects. These are composed into hetero-functional graphs via tensor-based incidence matrices that capture which resources can perform which processes on which operands. The paper introduces four linguistic fidelity properties (soundness, completeness, lucidity, laconicity) to assess model quality, and a meta-architecture that manages convergence between domain-specific reference architectures and case-specific instantiations. HFGT's structural grammar is explicitly designed to be domain-universal: the same operand categories and subject-predicate-object decomposition apply whether modeling water systems, energy grids, healthcare, or transportation.

What HFGT illuminates is substantial: it solves the problem of translating qualitative architectural descriptions into quantitative mathematical models without collapsing the heterogeneity that makes SoS modeling difficult. The tensor-based approach preserves structural distinctions that homogeneous network representations lose. The meta-architecture elegantly handles the convergence problem between abstract reference architectures and concrete implementations. What HFGT's own framing leaves implicit or unaddressed: (1) it has no account of why systems organize the way they do—it describes structure but not the forces or orientations that drive structural emergence; (2) the framework is purely structural-functional with no evaluative or orientational dimension—there is no way within HFGT to distinguish a well-organized system from a pathologically organized one except by external performance criteria; (3) the five operand categories (matter, energy, organisms, information, money) are presented as foundational but their selection is justified empirically rather than derived from any deeper structural principle; (4) the framework treats system boundaries and decomposition as given by the modeler rather than as emergent properties of the system itself.

### Native Reading

The paper addresses a well-known limitation in computational fluid dynamics: Reynolds-averaged Navier–Stokes (RANS) turbulence closures based on the Boussinesq eddy-viscosity hypothesis fail to accurately predict mean flow and turbulence statistics, particularly for complex geometries like flow past a cylinder. The authors assemble a cross-validated dataset spanning multiple experimental modalities (hydrodynamic PIV in a towing tank, aerodynamic PIV in a wind tunnel) and high-fidelity simulations (DNS, LES) across Reynolds numbers 3900–100,000 and Mach numbers 0–0.3. Their key empirical finding is a 'universal distribution' of Reynolds stresses across this parameter space—a structural invariance that persists despite regime differences. They then use physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), trained on the unclosed RANS equations with boundary information alone, to infer velocity fields and Reynolds-stress forcing. The resulting closure, embedded in both PINN solvers and conventional OpenFOAM solvers, significantly outperforms traditional models.

The paper's own framing illuminates several things clearly: the inadequacy of the Boussinesq assumption (linear stress-strain relationship) for non-canonical flows, the power of cross-validation across experimental modalities to establish data reliability, and the capacity of PINNs to serve as both inference engines and closure models by embedding physics constraints directly into the loss function. What the framing leaves implicit or underexplored: (1) the epistemological status of the 'universal distribution'—is it a deep structural property of cylinder wake turbulence or an artifact of the parameter range examined? (2) the relationship between the physics constraints and the data-driven components within the PINN—the paper treats these as a unified methodology but does not structurally analyze the tension between them or characterize when one dominates the other; (3) the failure modes—the paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., near-wall regions, higher Mach numbers) but does not structurally characterize why certain regimes resist the approach while others yield to it; (4) the motivational architecture of the research program itself—why this particular problem, this particular combination of methods, and what the implicit assumptions are about what constitutes 'improvement' in RANS predictions.
