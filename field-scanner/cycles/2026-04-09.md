### Native Reading

The Joching Manifesto proposes Spiritual Knowledge Management (SpKM) as a paradigm shift within knowledge management, moving from instrumental/transactional views of knowledge toward an integrative framework that foregrounds spiritual, ethical, cognitive, and aesthetic dimensions. Its 12 principles—including relational resonance, transformative spaces (Ba and Ma), caring, future-orientedness, phronesis (practical wisdom), aesthetic knowing, and agency—are distilled from collaborative scholarly dialogue rather than empirical research. The framework's structural primitives are heterogeneous: it draws on Nonaka's Ba (shared context for knowledge creation), Japanese Ma (negative/interstitial space), Aristotelian phronesis, contemplative/spiritual traditions, and aesthetic theory. The manifesto positions knowing as a transformative process directed toward the 'future best version' of individuals and collectives, explicitly rejecting knowledge-as-asset models.

What the paper illuminates: It names dimensions of organizational knowing that mainstream KM literature systematically excludes—spirituality, aesthetics, caring, and the transformation of the knower rather than the transfer of knowledge. It provides a rich vocabulary for the relational and orientational aspects of knowledge work. It is genuinely interdisciplinary, drawing on philosophy, theology, organizational science, psychology, and aesthetics.

What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit: The 12 principles are presented as a flat list without structural hierarchy, ordering, or explicit interdependencies. The manifesto does not articulate which principles are foundational vs. derivative, whether some principles enable or constrain others, or how conflicts between principles (e.g., future-orientedness vs. caring for present needs, aesthetic knowing vs. ethical demands) are resolved. The framework lacks any formal mechanism for distinguishing when its principles are operating constructively vs. when they might organize toward limitation—there is no shadow theory. The relationship between the spiritual, ethical, cognitive, and aesthetic 'dimensions' is asserted as integrative but never structurally specified: how these dimensions interact, whether they are independent or reducible, and what happens when they conflict are all left implicit. The paper is also entirely conceptual—no empirical validation, no formal model, no testable predictions beyond programmatic calls for future research.

### Native Reading

EPO presents a unified geometric framework for stabilizing high-order numerical methods (finite volume and discontinuous Galerkin) by consolidating three previously separate concerns: entropy stability, positivity/bound preservation, and oscillation suppression. The paper's central insight is that all three stabilization mechanisms act on the same cellwise candidate state and can be understood as constraints along a single geometric ray anchored at the updated cell average. Each constraint defines an admissibility radius on that ray, and the applied limiter is simply their minimum. The decisive analytical contribution is 'weak entropy stability' at the cell-average level—a two-point Lax-Friedrichs/Riemann-average entropy inequality that provides local cell-average entropy budgets, which the radial scaling mechanism then lifts to strong quadrature-based entropy inequalities. The framework handles any prescribed finite family of convex entropy pairs simultaneously by taking the minimum of their individual entropy radii.

What the paper's own framing illuminates is remarkable: the historical separation of these three stabilization concerns was an artifact of methodological tradition, not structural necessity. The geometric unification reveals that admissibility, entropy control, and oscillation suppression are not independent problems but projections of a single constraint geometry onto the ray from cell average to candidate state. The paper proves preservation of cell averages, invariant-set preservation, local and global entropy inequalities, and extensions to multidimensional meshes. What the paper's framing leaves implicit is WHY these three concerns were historically separated in the first place—it treats the separation as a historical accident rather than investigating whether there is a structural reason these three particular concerns (and not others) constitute the natural decomposition of numerical stabilization. The paper also does not address why the cell average serves as the natural anchor point beyond its mathematical convenience, nor does it explore whether the minimum-radius operation has deeper structural significance beyond being the tightest constraint.

### DN Engagement

### Native Reading

The paper introduces cross-regional brain entropy (CRBEN) as a novel biomarker for Alzheimer's disease progression, computed from resting-state fMRI data across 300 cortical regions using the Schaefer atlas. The core methodological innovation is applying cross-entropy (an information-theoretic measure of how well one region's signal distribution predicts another's) to characterize inter-regional information flow, then decomposing the resulting 300×300 CRBEN matrices into functional gradients via diffusion map embedding (BrainSpace). The paper finds progressive gradient flattening from NC→MCI→AD, with specific network-level signatures: somatomotor, ventral-attention, and default-mode networks show reduced gradients in MCI; further reductions in somatomotor and default-mode networks characterize the MCI→AD transition; and frontoparietal-control networks show significant reduction only in the NC→AD comparison. Logistic regression classifiers using gradient features achieve ~90% accuracy. The paper frames its contribution as demonstrating that CRBEN gradients capture 'progressive loss of network complexity' and 'reduced hierarchical organization' across the AD continuum.

What the paper's framing illuminates well: it establishes that cross-entropy (asymmetric, directional information flow) captures something structurally different from traditional functional connectivity (symmetric correlation), and that gradient decomposition of this asymmetric matrix reveals hierarchical organization that flattens with disease progression. The gradient approach is genuinely informative—it reduces a high-dimensional connectivity matrix to a low-dimensional organizational signature that tracks disease stage.

What the paper's framing misses or leaves implicit: (1) The paper treats gradient flattening as a unitary phenomenon—'loss of hierarchical organization'—without distinguishing whether different networks lose hierarchy for structurally different reasons or through different mechanisms. The default-mode network's gradient reduction could reflect fundamentally different organizational breakdown than the somatomotor network's, but the paper's framework treats them as instances of the same process at different thresholds. (2) The asymmetry of cross-entropy is noted but not structurally exploited—the paper computes CRBEN matrices but does not analyze the directionality of information flow disruption (which regions lose their capacity to predict others vs. which become unpredictable). (3) The paper does not address what the residual gradient structure in AD represents—is the remaining organization a degraded version of normal hierarchy, or has it reorganized into a qualitatively different configuration? (4) The relationship between gradient flattening and the patient's experiential/cognitive decline is left entirely implicit; the biomarker is treated as a measurement tool without structural interpretation of what the measured disintegration means for the system's functional architecture.
