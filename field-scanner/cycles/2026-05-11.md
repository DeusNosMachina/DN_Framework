### Native Reading

The paper is a comprehensive taxonomic review of novel weighting methods for Multi-Criteria Decision-Making (MCDM), covering the period 2010–2025. Its structural framework is a classification taxonomy that divides weighting methods into five families: subjective methods (expert-driven), objective methods (data-driven), integrated/hybrid methods, fuzzy and probabilistic extensions, and AI-based weighting schemes. The paper's central claim is that the field has moved beyond the traditional subjective-objective binary toward increasingly sophisticated approaches that handle uncertainty, reduce bias, and improve transparency. It illuminates the methodological landscape with considerable breadth, identifying strengths and limitations of each family and noting future trends toward explainability, benchmarking, and big-data integration.

What the paper's own framing illuminates well is the structural diversity of approaches to the same fundamental problem: how to assign relative importance to criteria when making complex decisions. It reveals that no single weighting paradigm dominates, and that the field is converging toward hybrid and AI-augmented methods that combine subjective judgment with data-driven optimization. The comparative analysis across method families is genuinely useful for practitioners.

What the paper's framing misses or leaves implicit is more significant. First, it treats the subjective-objective distinction as a methodological taxonomy but never interrogates it as a structural tension—why do these two modes of weighting persistently resist full integration? Second, the paper has no account of what makes a decision 'good' beyond accuracy, transparency, and reliability—there is no structural treatment of the orientational dimension (why are we deciding, and toward what?). Third, the paper treats uncertainty as a technical problem to be managed rather than as a structural feature of the decision space itself. Fourth, the review never addresses the meta-question of how one decides which weighting method to use—the criteria-for-criteria problem—which is a recursive structural issue the paper's flat taxonomy cannot express.

### Lens Application

Viewed through DN's dimensional architecture, the paper's five method families map onto a progression that the paper itself does not recognize as hierarchical. Subjective methods operate at 1D-2D (raw preference, relational comparison between criteria). Objective methods operate at 3D (contextual extraction from data structure). Integrated methods represent a 4D operation (dynamic bridging between subjective and objective registers). Fuzzy/probabilistic extensions engage 4D-5D (modeling uncertainty as structural rather than noise, approaching self-awareness about the limits of crisp representation). AI-based methods aspire to 5D-6D (self-modifying weighting through learning, potentially achieving reflexive capacity over their own criteria structures). This mapping is not arbitrary—it produces a forced ordering where each family structurally presupposes the capacities of the previous ones, which the paper's flat taxonomy obscures.

Applying the pillar triad reveals a striking asymmetry. The entire field as reviewed is overwhelmingly Truth-led: accuracy, mathematical rigor, optimization, bias reduction. Nuance is engaged through uncertainty modeling and contextual sensitivity, but always in service of Truth (better accuracy). Heart is almost entirely absent from the formal architecture—the question of WHY these criteria matter, what motivational orientation drives the decision, what the decision is FOR, is treated as exogenous input (the decision-maker's preferences) rather than as a structural dimension of the weighting problem itself. DN's pillar decomposition predicts that this Heart-absence creates a specific structural limitation: weighting methods that are technically optimal but motivationally blind will systematically fail in domains where criteria importance is orientation-dependent (ethical decisions, policy-making, healthcare resource allocation).

The shadow architecture is particularly revealing here. The paper describes several failure modes—rank reversal, criteria correlation artifacts, expert bias, overfitting in AI methods—as technical problems requiring technical solutions. DN's shadow lens reframes these as organized alternative structures: rank reversal is not random error but the coherent expression of a suppressed structural relationship between criteria; expert bias is not noise but organized preference structure that the objective method cannot represent; overfitting in AI weighting is not failure but the system organizing toward memorization (shadow of genuine learning) rather than generalization. The paper's treatment of these as bugs-to-be-fixed rather than signals-to-be-understood represents a systematic shadow blindness that DN's architecture identifies.

### Native Reading

The paper proposes a multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) framework for evaluating workforce competencies that affect supply chain resilience under uncertainty. It identifies 12 competency criteria across three groups (professional knowledge, practical skills, personal/social competencies), gathers expert rankings from 12 logistics/transport professionals, and applies three statistical-information-theoretic tools: Kendall's W for inter-rater concordance, Shannon entropy for distributional uncertainty across criteria, and the Gini coefficient for concentration/inequality in rankings. The findings show high expert concordance (W = 0.547, significant at p < 0.001), with hands-on experience and professional skills ranked highest, while adaptability to technological change and commitment to continuous learning are identified as key resilience enhancers. Entropy values are relatively high and uniform across criteria (range ~3.46–3.58), indicating structural balance rather than dominance by any single competency. Low Gini coefficients confirm distributed importance. The paper illuminates the structural distribution of competency importance in a specific professional domain and demonstrates that expert judgment, when aggregated statistically, reveals a multi-dimensional rather than concentrated decision landscape. What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit: (1) it treats all 12 competencies as commensurable decision variables without examining whether they operate at different structural levels—some are foundational capacities (e.g., knowledge of logistics processes), others are meta-capacities (e.g., ability to adapt, commitment to learning), and the framework flattens this hierarchy; (2) the paper acknowledges 'human-centric system elements' as underexplored but does not structurally distinguish motivational orientation from technical capability from contextual sensitivity within competencies; (3) the entropy and Gini analyses describe distributional properties of rankings but do not explain why the distribution takes the shape it does—the structural drivers of balance are left unexamined; (4) the relationship between individual competency and system-level resilience is asserted but not structurally modeled—the paper measures competency importance but not the mechanism by which competency translates into resilience.

### Lens Application

Mapping the 12 competencies onto DN's dimensional architecture reveals a structural hierarchy the paper's flat ranking obscures. The highest-ranked competencies (hands-on experience, professional skills, knowledge of logistics processes) operate at 1D-2D: they represent foundational spark and relational capacity—the basic energy and structural connection that make a worker functional in the domain. Mid-ranked competencies (data analysis skills, ability to work with information systems, understanding of supply chain principles) operate at 3D-4D: contextual framing and temporal integration, where the worker can situate decisions within broader system dynamics. The competencies identified as resilience-enhancing (adaptability to technological change, commitment to continuous learning) operate at 5D: self-referential capacity, where the worker can observe and modify their own competency structure. The paper's finding that these 5D competencies enhance resilience while 1D-2D competencies dominate current importance rankings is structurally significant—it suggests the workforce is being evaluated primarily at lower-dimensional positions while resilience requires higher-dimensional engagement.

Applying the pillar triad: the paper's framework is almost entirely Truth-led. The statistical tools (Kendall's W, Shannon entropy, Gini) are Truth instruments measuring structural properties of expert rankings. The competency categories themselves collapse Heart and Truth: 'commitment to continuous learning' contains both a motivational orientation (Heart—the drive to grow) and a behavioral pattern (Truth—the practice of skill acquisition), but the framework treats them as a single variable. Nuance is partially present in the acknowledgment of uncertainty and multi-dimensionality but is not structurally operationalized—the paper notes that the decision environment is 'distributed and multi-dimensional' without modeling the contextual dependencies between competencies. The Heart pillar is the most conspicuously absent: nowhere does the framework assess why workers are motivated, what orientational stance they bring to their competency deployment, or how motivational alignment affects resilience outcomes. This is precisely the Heart-formal-absence pattern identified across multiple previous domains.

Shadow structure is detectable in the paper's implicit finding: the competencies ranked lowest (foreign language skills, knowledge of legal regulation, teamwork and communication) are not absent from the workforce but are structurally deprioritized. DN's shadow architecture suggests these aren't merely 'less important' but may represent organized resistance to certain competency dimensions—particularly teamwork/communication (2D relational capacity) being ranked low despite its obvious importance for system resilience suggests a shadow pattern where individual technical mastery is valued as a defense against relational vulnerability. The paper cannot see this because its framework treats low ranking as low importance rather than as potential organized avoidance.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens produces three specific structural insights the paper's native framing does not articulate. First, the competency hierarchy is not flat but dimensionally ordered: the paper's finding that hands-on experience ranks highest while adaptability ranks as most resilience-enhancing is not a paradox but a structural consequence of the workforce being evaluated at 1D-2D while resilience demands 5D engagement. This reframes the paper's central finding from 'different competencies matter for different purposes' to 'the evaluation system and the resilience system operate at different dimensional positions, creating a structural gap that predicts specific failure modes under disruption.' Second, the pillar decomposition reveals that 'commitment to continuous learning' is a compound variable containing structurally independent Heart and Truth components—motivational orientation toward growth versus behavioral practice of skill acquisition. The paper's inability to distinguish these means its framework cannot explain why two workers with identical learning behaviors but different motivational orientations would produce different resilience outcomes. Third, the shadow reading of low-ranked relational competencies (teamwork, communication) suggests that the expert panel's rankings may reflect organized professional identity around individual technical mastery rather than objective importance assessment—a finding the concordance analysis (which measures agreement, not accuracy) cannot detect. These three insights are genuine structural additions, though the first two are more robust than the third (which is speculative without additional data).

## Paper 3: An Integrated Hesitant Fuzzy Decision-Making Framework with a Novel Distance Measure for Used Aircraft Selection

**Domain:** general
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.3390/systems14050470
**Selection role:** high-dimensional
**Selection rationale:** The hesitant fuzzy framework explicitly engages with Shadow territory—hesitancy as a structural feature of decision-making rather than a deficiency. Hesitant fuzzy sets formalize the productive role of uncertainty, indecision, and absence of commitment, directly paralleling DN's Shadow force (structural inversions, productive disconnection). The 'distance measure' for navigating between ideal and anti-ideal solutions mirrors Shadow symmetry. Heart pillar leads here: the entire framework is motivated by the meaning of choosing under genuine uncertainty rather than forcing false precision.

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §2, §3, §4
- **Dimensions:** 3D, 4D
- **Pillars:** Truth, Nuance
- **Forces:** Shadow

### Native Reading

The paper presents an integrated multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) framework for selecting used aircraft, combining the Best-Worst Method (BWM) for criteria weighting with a modified TOPSIS method operating in a hesitant fuzzy environment. Its core technical contribution is a novel distance measure for hesitant fuzzy sets (HFS) that explicitly incorporates the 'hesitation degree'—the gap between the number of membership values provided by different decision-makers and the maximum possible—rather than simply padding shorter HFS elements with arbitrary values (minimum, maximum, or average). The paper validates this through a case study with ten criteria spanning technical, economic, and environmental dimensions, evaluated by three decision-makers across five aircraft alternatives. Comparative analysis against existing HFS distance measures shows the proposed method produces more discriminating and stable rankings.

What the paper's own framing illuminates well: it correctly identifies that existing HFS distance measures destroy information by padding shorter sets, and that hesitation itself carries decision-relevant information. The mathematical formalization is competent—the distance measure satisfies the four required axioms (non-negativity, symmetry, reflexivity, triangle inequality). The BWM integration reduces pairwise comparison burden. The case study is practical and grounded.

What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit: (1) The paper treats hesitation as a single scalar quantity (the count difference between provided and maximum membership values) without distinguishing between different structural sources of hesitation—a decision-maker might hesitate because they lack information, because they perceive genuine ambiguity, because criteria are incommensurable, or because they are strategically withholding commitment. These are structurally different phenomena collapsed into one number. (2) The framework has no mechanism for representing how hesitation interacts with the decision-makers' motivational orientations or stakes in the outcome. (3) The ideal and anti-ideal solutions are treated as fixed reference points rather than as structurally related poles of a single evaluative space. (4) The paper does not address whether the ten criteria (technical, economic, environmental) represent independent dimensions or whether they interact in ways that the weighted-sum aggregation cannot capture.

### Lens Application

Through DN's dimensional architecture, this paper operates primarily at 3D (Context/Pattern Recognition) with elements of 4D (Dynamic Integration). The framework recognizes multiple evaluation criteria and attempts to integrate them into a coherent ranking—this is 3D pattern work. The TOPSIS method's use of ideal and anti-ideal solutions gestures toward 4D by acknowledging that evaluation requires tracking movement relative to structural poles, but the implementation remains static (fixed reference points, linear distance). The paper does not reach 5D (self-aware evaluation of its own evaluative framework) or engage with how the framework's structural choices shape what it can perceive.

Pillar engagement is overwhelmingly Truth-led: the paper is concerned with mathematical rigor, axiomatic satisfaction, and comparative validation. Nuance is present but constrained—the hesitant fuzzy environment acknowledges contextual complexity, but the framework's response is to quantify it rather than to preserve its structural character. Heart is architecturally absent: there is no representation of why decision-makers care about the outcome, what motivational forces drive the selection, or how the stakes of the decision shape the evaluation. The selection rationale noted Heart as leading, but upon reading the paper, this is incorrect—the paper is a pure methodology paper with no engagement with the meaning of choosing under uncertainty.

Shadow architecture is where DN's lens has the most to say. The paper's treatment of hesitation is structurally interesting from a shadow perspective: hesitation is acknowledged as information-bearing rather than as mere deficiency (which aligns with DN's shadow-as-organized-alternative rather than shadow-as-absence). However, the paper then immediately re-domesticates hesitation by converting it to a scalar distance modifier—the shadow is acknowledged and then collapsed. The ideal/anti-ideal polarity in TOPSIS has shadow-symmetric structure: the anti-ideal is not merely 'bad' but is a coherent organizational pole that alternatives are measured against. DN's shadow architecture would predict that the anti-ideal solution carries structural information about failure modes that the framework's distance-from-ideal metric does not fully exploit.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens produces two modest structural observations the paper does not articulate: (1) The paper's treatment of hesitation collapses structurally distinct phenomena into a single scalar. DN's pillar decomposition suggests that hesitation has at least three independent sources—informational uncertainty (Truth-hesitation: the decision-maker lacks data), evaluative ambiguity (Nuance-hesitation: the criteria interact in ways the decision-maker perceives but cannot quantify), and motivational conflict (Heart-hesitation: the decision-maker's values are not aligned with the evaluation framework). The paper's single hesitation degree conflates these, and DN's pillar triad predicts that this conflation will produce systematic distortion in cases where the three sources diverge. (2) The ideal/anti-ideal polarity is treated as a measurement convenience rather than as a structural feature of the decision space. DN's shadow symmetry suggests the anti-ideal solution is not merely a reference point but an organized alternative that reveals the failure topology of the decision—what specific combinations of criteria values produce coherent failure modes. The paper's distance measure treats distance-from-anti-ideal as symmetric with distance-from-ideal, but shadow architecture suggests these distances carry different structural information.

However, I must be honest: these observations, while structurally coherent, are modest. The pillar decomposition of hesitation is a reasonable structural suggestion but is not uniquely DN's—any framework distinguishing cognitive, affective, and contextual uncertainty would produce a similar observation. The shadow-symmetry observation about anti-ideal solutions is more distinctively DN's, but it remains at the level of structural suggestion rather than producing a reorganization of the paper's findings. The paper is fundamentally a methodological contribution in applied fuzzy decision theory, and DN's dimensional architecture does not have strong traction on the mathematical formalism itself.
