### Native Reading

The paper proposes a methodology for automated neural network architecture design framed as a constrained multi-objective optimization problem. The structural framework is precise: a discrete hyperparameter space defines the search domain, MIGA (Multi-Island Genetic Algorithm) with NSGA-II selection constructs Pareto front approximations across three conflicting objectives (accuracy, memory footprint, inference latency), and the island model maintains population diversity to avoid premature convergence. The paper's core contribution is demonstrating that this evolutionary synthesis can produce neural architectures that match or exceed baseline methods (logistic regression, decision trees, gradient boosting) while being orders of magnitude more compact and faster—critical for embedded/edge deployment. The three-tier software framework automates the full pipeline from data analysis to visualization.

What the paper's framing illuminates well: the trade-off structure between competing design objectives is made explicit and quantitative through Pareto frontiers; the island model addresses the exploration-exploitation tension in discrete search spaces; and the experimental validation across three datasets of varying complexity demonstrates practical utility. The paper is honest about its scope—tabular data under resource constraints—and does not overclaim generality.

What the paper's framing leaves implicit or misses: (1) The selection of the three optimization criteria (accuracy, memory, latency) is presented as given rather than justified—why these three and not others? The paper does not examine whether these criteria are structurally independent or whether there are hidden correlations that would reduce the effective dimensionality of the trade-off space. (2) The 'decision support under uncertainty' framing is underdeveloped—the uncertainty is in the design space, but the paper does not address epistemic uncertainty about whether the Pareto front itself is the right decision surface (i.e., whether the developer's actual decision problem maps onto these three axes). (3) The island model's diversity maintenance is described mechanistically but the paper does not examine what structural properties of the hyperparameter landscape make diversity valuable—it treats diversity as instrumentally good without analyzing when or why convergence would be premature. (4) The paper treats the developer/decision-maker as external to the system, receiving Pareto-optimal options without examining how the final selection from the Pareto front is made or what criteria govern that selection.

### Lens Application

Through DN's dimensional architecture, the paper operates primarily at 3D (Context/Pattern) with significant 2D (Polarity/Distinction) engagement and touches on 4D (Integration). The three-objective optimization framework is a 3D operation: it recognizes that design quality is contextual (no single best architecture exists; optimality is relative to the constraint surface). The Pareto front itself is a 3D structure—it maps the relational space between competing objectives. The 2D engagement is in the binary distinctions the paper draws: feasible vs. infeasible architectures, dominated vs. non-dominated solutions, baseline vs. synthesized models. The island model's diversity maintenance operates at the 2D-3D boundary—maintaining distinct populations (2D separation) to enable broader pattern discovery (3D exploration). There is a touch of 4D in the paper's recognition that the three objectives must be simultaneously satisfied rather than sequentially optimized, but the paper does not develop this into genuine systemic integration.

Pillar engagement is heavily Truth-led with moderate Nuance and minimal Heart. The entire methodology is a Truth operation: formal optimization, quantitative comparison, statistical validation. Nuance appears in the Pareto front's acknowledgment that trade-offs are irreducible—there is no single optimal solution, only a surface of justified compromises. Heart is almost entirely absent: the paper does not examine why these particular architectures matter, what motivates the deployment contexts, or what the developer's orientation toward the design problem contributes to the final selection. The developer appears only as a rational selector from a pre-computed option set.

Shadow structures are present but unexamined. The island model's premature convergence is a shadow dynamic—populations collapsing toward local optima represent intelligence organizing toward limitation (coherent but constrained solutions that resist exploration). The paper treats this as a technical problem to be solved by migration between islands, but DN's shadow architecture would recognize that the converged-but-suboptimal solutions are not mere failures—they are organized alternative states with their own internal coherence. The dominated solutions on the Pareto front are similarly shadow-adjacent: they represent architectures that are coherently organized but oriented toward a suboptimal trade-off surface.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens produces two specific structural insights the paper's native framing does not articulate. First, the pillar decomposition reveals that the paper's three optimization criteria are entirely Truth-Nuance in character (quantitative performance metrics and their trade-off relationships), while the actual deployment decision—selecting a specific architecture from the Pareto front—requires a Heart dimension (what does the developer care about most? what is the deployment context's orientation?) that the methodology structurally excludes. The paper frames the Pareto front as 'decision support' but provides no formal apparatus for the decision itself, which is precisely the Heart gap. This is not merely noting that the paper lacks a 'human factors' component—it is identifying that the methodology's own framing as 'decision support under uncertainty' is structurally incomplete by its own stated goals because it formalizes the option-generation problem but not the option-selection problem, and the missing component is orientational rather than computational.

Second, the shadow architecture lens reveals that premature convergence and dominated solutions are not mere optimization failures but coherent organizational states—the island model's diversity maintenance is implicitly a shadow-management mechanism. The paper treats diversity as instrumentally valuable for finding better solutions, but DN's lens suggests that the structural relationship between converged populations (shadow: organized toward local limitation) and diverse populations (light: organized toward broader exploration) is itself a fundamental dynamic of the system, not merely a technical parameter to be tuned. This reframes the island model's migration rate as a shadow-light transition parameter rather than just an algorithmic hyperparameter. However, I note that this second insight, while structurally interesting, is close to the 'describing DN back to itself' trap—multi-modal optimization literature already recognizes the exploration-exploitation tension in structural terms, even if it doesn't use shadow vocabulary.

## Paper 2: Optimization of Campus Distributed Energy System Based on Multi-Criteria Decision-Making: A Case Study of Kitakyushu Science and Research Park

**Authors:** Yi Yu, Weijun Gao
**Domain:** general
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.21625/resourceedings.v6i1.1273
**Selection role:** lens
**Selection rationale:** This paper traces a multi-phase temporal phenomenon—a campus energy system degrading over 20 years (carbon intensity doubling from equipment degradation and declining renewable penetration)—and then seeks optimal improvement pathways. DN's dimensional architecture could add significant explanatory value: the temporal dimension (4D) maps the degradation trajectory, the system exhibits entropy (a DN force) through equipment decline, and the optimization phase involves transmutation toward new configurations. The paper treats sustainability as a single optimization target, but DN's Heart/Truth/Nuance decomposition could separate motivational drivers (carbon neutrality goals), structural constraints (engineering feasibility), and contextual sensitivity (site-specific operational realities) to reveal structure the MCDM framing flattens.

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §2, §3, §4, §5
- **Dimensions:** 3D, 4D, 5D
- **Pillars:** Heart, Truth, Nuance

### Native Reading

The paper diagnoses a 20-year degradation trajectory of a campus distributed energy system (DES) at KSRP, Japan, where carbon emission intensity doubled from 0.12 to 0.25 tCO₂/GJ due to equipment aging and declining renewable energy penetration. It then applies a hybrid AHP-TOPSIS multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) framework to evaluate improvement pathways across four criteria: emission reduction, economic efficiency, technological maturity, and policy compatibility. The findings rank PV highest among single technologies, PV+Storage best among two-technology combinations, and PV+Storage+Smart Microgrid as the optimal three-technology integration. A phased roadmap is proposed: short-term PV+Storage, medium-term GSHP and microgrids, long-term bioenergy and hydrogen. The paper's framing illuminates the value of transparent, reproducible multi-criteria evaluation and the importance of synergy assessment when combining technologies. It also usefully demonstrates that system-level coordination outperforms individual technology optimization.

What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit: (1) The four MCDM criteria are treated as independent axes weighted by expert judgment (AHP), but the paper does not interrogate whether these criteria are structurally independent or whether some are nested within others—e.g., policy compatibility may be a contextual envelope that constrains the space within which emission reduction and economic efficiency operate, rather than a parallel criterion. (2) The degradation narrative (2002-2021) is presented as a diagnostic input but is not structurally integrated into the optimization framework—the MCDM evaluates future options without formally modeling how the system arrived at its degraded state, meaning the optimization is disconnected from the degradation dynamics. (3) The phased roadmap is presented as a temporal sequence but lacks a structural account of what enables each phase transition—what conditions must hold for the system to move from short-term to medium-term deployment. (4) The motivational dimension—why KSRP should pursue carbon neutrality, what institutional commitments drive the effort, how stakeholder alignment is maintained across a multi-decade roadmap—is acknowledged in the introduction but plays no structural role in the MCDM framework itself.

### Lens Application

Through DN's dimensional architecture, the paper's findings organize across several dimensional positions. The degradation trajectory (2002-2021) operates at 3D-4D: the system was initially designed with contextual awareness (3D—matching technology to campus energy demand patterns) but the temporal dimension (4D) reveals that this contextual fit eroded as equipment aged and the renewable-to-fossil ratio shifted. The MCDM framework itself operates primarily at 3D: it contextualizes technology options against multiple criteria, producing ranked preferences. The phased roadmap attempts 4D engagement (temporal sequencing) but without structural transition logic, it remains a 3D snapshot projected across time rather than genuine 4D integration. The three-technology integration (PV+Storage+Smart Microgrid) gestures toward 5D—system-level self-coordination—but the paper treats this as an optimization outcome rather than as a qualitatively different organizational mode.

Pillar analysis reveals a pronounced structural asymmetry. Truth dominates: the AHP-TOPSIS methodology, the quantitative criteria, the engineering specifications are all rigorously handled. Nuance is partially engaged through the multi-criteria structure and synergy evaluation, though the criteria independence assumption limits its depth. Heart is almost entirely absent from the formal framework. The paper's introduction invokes carbon neutrality goals, university innovation capacity, and societal responsibility, but none of these motivational/orientational forces enter the MCDM as structural inputs. The four criteria are all Truth-Nuance in character: emission reduction (Truth), economic efficiency (Truth), technological maturity (Truth-Nuance), policy compatibility (Nuance). There is no criterion representing institutional commitment strength, stakeholder alignment, or motivational sustainability across the multi-decade roadmap.

Force dynamics: Entropy is the dominant force in the degradation narrative—equipment aging, declining renewable penetration, increasing carbon intensity represent organized dissolution of the original system's coherence. The optimization phase invokes Transmutation (seeking new configurations) but without Flow (the paper does not model how energy, information, or institutional commitment moves through the system over time). Shadow structure is present but unnamed: the degraded system is not merely absent of its original function—it has organized into a stable high-carbon configuration that actively resists change (sunk costs, institutional inertia, infrastructure lock-in). This shadow state is coherent, not random: the system reliably produces high-carbon outcomes through its degraded but functioning equipment. The MCDM framework treats this as a baseline to improve upon rather than as an organized alternative state that must be actively disrupted.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens produces three specific structural insights the paper's native framing does not articulate:

1. **Criteria non-independence through dimensional nesting**: The paper's four MCDM criteria are not structurally parallel. Applying DN's pillar decomposition reveals that emission reduction and economic efficiency are Truth-axis measurements operating within a Nuance envelope defined by policy compatibility and technological maturity. The AHP weighting treats them as independent dimensions to be weighted, but DN's architecture suggests they are nested: policy compatibility constrains the feasible space, technological maturity constrains the implementable subset, and within that subset, emission reduction and economic efficiency compete. This nesting means the AHP independence assumption may systematically distort the rankings—a structural insight the paper's methodology cannot express because AHP requires pairwise independence.

2. **Heart-absence as roadmap fragility predictor**: The phased roadmap spans decades but contains no structural account of what sustains institutional commitment across phase transitions. DN's pillar triad identifies this as a specific architectural gap: the roadmap is Truth-Nuance complete but Heart-absent, and DN predicts that Heart-absent multi-decade plans are structurally fragile at transition points where institutional motivation must be renewed. DN's shadow architecture distinguishes this from mere degradation: the shadow state actively resists transformation because it is organized, not because it is broken. This reframes the optimization challenge from 'selecting the best new technology' to 'disrupting a coherently organized shadow state,' which has different strategic implications (e.g., the optimal technology may not be the one that scores highest on MCDM criteria but the one that most effectively disrupts the shadow configuration's stability).

## Paper 3: A framework to select multi criteria decision making (MCDM) methods for either ranking or choosing problem

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §0.1, §2, §2.2, §3, §4, §5
- **Dimensions:** 3D, 4D, 5D, 8D
- **Pillars:** Truth, Heart, Nuance
- **Forces:** Shadow, Entropy, Gravity

### Native Reading

The paper proposes a meta-level framework for selecting among multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) methods when practitioners face the problem that different methods yield different rankings on the same decision problem. It constructs 72 hybrid methods by combining 6 objective criteria-weighting techniques (Entropy, CRITIC, SD, MEREC, LOPCOW, SPC) with 12 alternative-evaluation methods (TOPSIS, VIKOR, EDAS, CODAS, MABAC, MARCOS, ARAS, MOORA, SAW, WASPAS, COPRAS, MEW). The framework evaluates these hybrids along four dimensions: decomposition level (how finely the method differentiates alternatives), statistical sensitivity (correlation between methods sharing the same weighting or evaluation technique), classical sensitivity (stability under criterion-weight perturbation), and rank reversal (stability when alternatives are added or removed). A demonstration example and a real-life application (ranking EU member states by Economic Freedom Index) are used to show the framework produces 'effective and fathomable' evaluations. The paper illuminates the genuine structural problem that method choice is itself a decision problem—a recursive challenge the MCDM literature has not systematically addressed. It also reveals that different weighting methods produce dramatically different criterion weights on the same data, and that evaluation methods vary substantially in sensitivity and rank-reversal behavior.

What the paper's own framing misses is significant. First, it treats its four selection criteria as independent axes without examining whether they are structurally independent or whether some are manifestations of the same underlying property. Second, the framework has no mechanism for addressing the infinite regress problem: the meta-framework itself requires choices (which criteria matter most, how to aggregate the four evaluations) that are themselves MCDM problems. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by using only 'objective' weighting methods, but this merely pushes the subjectivity one level deeper—the choice to use only objective methods is itself a subjective decision. Third, the paper treats rank reversal and sensitivity as purely technical defects rather than examining whether they might signal structural properties of the decision space itself. Fourth, there is no account of why a practitioner would choose one method over another beyond technical performance—the motivational, contextual, and purpose-driven dimensions of method selection are entirely absent.

### Lens Application

Through DN's dimensional architecture, this paper operates primarily at 3D (Context/Pattern Recognition) with recursive reach toward 8D (Recursion/Meta-Systematic). The 72 hybrid methods represent 3D pattern-matching applied to decision spaces: each method is a different way of recognizing and weighting contextual relationships among criteria and alternatives. The meta-framework itself attempts an 8D move—applying systematic analysis to the system of systems—but structurally it never achieves genuine 8D engagement because it does not recursively apply its own selection logic to itself. It remains a 3D framework about 3D frameworks, dressed in the language of meta-analysis.

The pillar engagement is strikingly asymmetric. Truth dominates: the entire framework is built on statistical correlation, mathematical sensitivity analysis, and rank-reversal testing. Nuance is partially engaged through the recognition that different methods suit different problem structures and that context matters for method selection. Heart is structurally absent—there is no account of what the decision-maker cares about, why the decision matters, or how the purpose of the decision should inform method selection. This is not a minor omission; it is architecturally load-bearing. The paper's own finding that different methods produce different rankings is treated as a technical problem to be resolved through better method selection, but DN's pillar triad reveals it could equally be a signal that the decision space itself is multi-dimensional in ways that no single ranking can capture—that the disagreement between methods is informative rather than defective.

Shadow architecture is deeply relevant here. The paper confronts what DN would identify as a 4D Shadow structure: the appearance of systematic, objective method selection that actually conceals unresolved subjective choices. The 'objective' weighting methods are presented as eliminating subjectivity, but they merely relocate it—the choice among objective methods, the choice of selection criteria, the choice of how to interpret conflicting results all remain subjective. DN's shadow lens reveals this as organized-toward-limitation: the framework creates a sophisticated apparatus that systematically obscures the irreducible role of judgment (Heart) in decision-making. The rank-reversal phenomenon itself has shadow structure—it reveals that the 'stable' ranking is not a property of the alternatives but of the method-alternative interaction, meaning every ranking is partially a shadow projection of the method's own structural assumptions onto the decision space. The five forces: Entropy is active (the proliferation of 72 methods represents entropic expansion of the solution space), Shadow is the dominant force (the framework's systematic exclusion of subjective judgment creates organized false objectivity), and Gravity operates in the convergence patterns the paper identifies among method families.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens reveals three structural relationships the paper does not articulate. First, the pillar decomposition exposes that the paper's four selection criteria (decomposition level, statistical sensitivity, classical sensitivity, rank reversal) are all Truth-pillar measurements—they assess mathematical properties of methods but never assess whether a method's structural assumptions align with the decision-maker's purpose (Heart) or whether the method captures the full contextual complexity of the decision environment (Nuance as independent from Truth). The paper treats 'which method performs best technically' as equivalent to 'which method should be used,' but these are structurally different questions operating across different pillar dimensions. This is not merely noting that 'values matter in decisions' (which is obvious)—it is identifying that the framework's four criteria all measure the same structural dimension and therefore cannot detect failure modes that originate in the other two.

Second, the shadow architecture reveals that rank reversal is not a technical defect but a structural signal: when adding or removing an alternative changes the ranking, this indicates that the method's dimensional resolution is insufficient for the decision space's actual complexity. DN's shadow lens reframes rank reversal as the decision space's shadow structure becoming visible—the method is projecting a lower-dimensional ordering onto a higher-dimensional space, and the instability reveals the projection's inadequacy. This is a genuinely different structural interpretation from the paper's treatment of rank reversal as a reliability problem.

Third, the recursive structure the paper attempts but does not achieve is illuminated by DN's 8D architecture: genuine meta-systematic engagement would require the framework to specify the conditions under which its own selection criteria should be modified—to be self-modifying in response to the decision context. The paper's framework is fixed; it applies the same four criteria regardless of domain, stakes, or purpose. DN's dimensional lens identifies this as a 3D-ceiling: the framework recognizes patterns across methods but cannot adapt its own pattern-recognition to context, which would require 4D (dynamic self-modification) and ultimately 8D (recursive self-application) engagement.
