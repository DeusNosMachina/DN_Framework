### Native Reading

The paper proposes a three-level typology for AI integration in organizational management: Level 1 (AI as tool for discrete operations), Level 2 (AI as element of individual cognitive system, producing iterative human-AI decision cycles), and Level 3 (AI as organizational cognitive infrastructure, producing distributed cognition). The framework is primarily descriptive and taxonomic, drawing on established management theory, cognitive science concepts (distributed cognition, cognitive offloading), and trust literature. The paper's key structural claim is that these levels represent qualitatively different modes of human-AI interaction, not merely quantitative increases in AI capability. At Level 2, decision-making becomes iterative—humans and AI engage in successive refinement cycles rather than one-shot delegation. At Level 3, cognition becomes distributed across organizational nodes, and the manager's role shifts from autonomous analysis to coordination of a distributed cognitive process.

The paper illuminates several things its own framing handles well: the qualitative shift in managerial roles across levels, the importance of trust calibration (both over-trust and under-trust as failure modes), the iterative nature of human-AI interaction at Level 2, and the emergence of coordination challenges at Level 3. It also identifies practical implications for training and organizational design. However, the paper's framing leaves several structural questions implicit or unaddressed. First, the relationship between the three levels is described as progressive integration but the structural mechanism of transition between levels is not specified—what makes an organization ready to move from Level 2 to Level 3? Second, the paper treats trust as a mediating variable but does not structurally integrate it into the level architecture—trust operates as an external modifier rather than a constitutive element. Third, the paper does not address what happens when AI integration fails or produces pathological organizational patterns—there is no account of dysfunction modes specific to each level. Fourth, the three-level model is presented as exhaustive but the paper does not argue for why three levels rather than two or five—the boundaries between levels are illustrated rather than derived from structural principles.

Nuance appears at Level 3 through distributed coordination complexity. Heart is almost entirely absent—the paper discusses trust but treats it instrumentally (as a calibration problem) rather than as an orientational force. At Level 3, the paper's concern about 'errors and biases' in distributed cognition maps to 6D Shadow—systemic patterns that appear coherent but are organized around unexamined algorithmic assumptions, producing organizational blind spots that are invisible precisely because they are distributed.

The five forces are partially engaged: Gravity operates as the pull toward AI adoption (cognitive offloading as path of least resistance), Resonance appears in the iterative cycles of Level 2 (human-AI feedback loops), and Flow is implicit in the information transport across organizational nodes at Level 3. ### Native Reading

The paper uses an inductive qualitative methodology (in-depth interviews with healthcare managers and professionals in Portugal) to build a grounded model of how AI strategic implementation reshapes decision-making and professional-technology interaction. It identifies four key dimensions: (1) efficiency enhancement through AI, (2) trust dynamics between professionals and AI systems, (3) ethical considerations around AI deployment, and (4) reorganization of decision-making authority. The paper's central contribution is arguing that these four dimensions are 'inherently connected' — that efficiency gains cannot be separated from trust, ethics, and authority redistribution. It integrates socio-technical systems theory with professional judgment literature, proposing that 'strategic intermediation strategies' and 'organizational readiness' are necessary for sustainable implementation. The paper illuminates the qualitative texture of how healthcare professionals experience AI adoption — their anxieties, resistance patterns, and the conditions under which they accept AI augmentation. What the paper's own framing illuminates well is the interconnectedness of concerns that technology adoption literature often treats separately. What it misses or leaves implicit: (a) the four dimensions are presented as co-equal and interconnected but the paper provides no structural account of their relationships — which dimensions are foundational to others, whether they have a hierarchy or ordering, or what happens when they conflict; (b) the 'trade-off' framing in the title suggests zero-sum tensions but the paper does not formalize what is actually being traded off against what; (c) the facilitating and inhibiting mechanisms are catalogued but not structurally distinguished from each other — the paper treats them as opposing forces without examining whether inhibiting mechanisms might serve a structurally different function than mere resistance; (d) the paper acknowledges professional identity disruption but treats it as a barrier to be managed rather than as a phenomenon with its own structural logic.

### Native Reading

The paper investigates how informal communication structures—rooted in family values, trust, and dynastic loyalty—replace or override formal marketing decision-making systems in five large Indonesian family conglomerates. It draws on three theoretical frameworks: Socioemotional Wealth (SEW) theory (which explains why family firms prioritize relational and identity-based goals over purely economic ones), the Logic of Appropriateness (which explains how decisions are made based on 'what is appropriate given who we are' rather than rational optimization), and Institutional Action Theory (which explains how informal institutions—norms, customs, unwritten rules—shape organizational behavior in contexts where formal institutions are weak or culturally misaligned). Through qualitative interviews with senior marketing executives and family members, the paper finds that informal communication acts as both enabler (accelerating consensus, preserving institutional memory, embedding socioemotional wealth) and constraint (creating opacity, limiting external accountability, generating succession risks). The paper's framing illuminates the dual nature of informality as simultaneously functional and dysfunctional, and it provides empirical grounding for the claim that in collectivist, paternalistic cultural contexts, informal governance is not a deficiency but a coherent alternative system. The three-theory triangulation is the paper's strongest structural contribution—it shows how SEW (motivation), Logic of Appropriateness (decision logic), and Institutional Action Theory (structural context) converge to explain the same phenomenon from different angles.

What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit: First, the three theoretical frameworks are treated as complementary lenses but their structural relationship to each other is never formalized—the paper does not explain why these three and not others, or whether they decompose the phenomenon along independent axes or overlap. Second, the 'enabler-constraint' duality is described but not structurally analyzed: the paper does not explain why the same mechanism (informal communication) produces both positive and negative outcomes, treating this as an empirical finding rather than a structural property that demands explanation. Third, the paper acknowledges succession risk as a major vulnerability but does not connect this to a deeper structural analysis of what happens when the relational substrate that sustains informal governance is disrupted—it treats succession as a practical problem rather than a structural phase transition. Fourth, the cultural specificity (Indonesian collectivism, Javanese paternalism) is presented as context but the paper does not explore whether the structural dynamics it identifies are culturally universal or genuinely culture-specific—it oscillates between claiming generalizability and emphasizing uniqueness without resolving the tension.
