### Native Reading

The paper is a position paper drawing on an ongoing empirical study comparing two LLM-powered strategies for supporting minority voices in hierarchical group decision-making. The first strategy (Anonymous Relay, AR) anonymizes minority input by having the AI relay it without attribution. The second (Autonomous Counterargument, AC) has the AI independently generate counterarguments aligned with the minority position without using the minority member's actual words. The counterintuitive finding is that AR—the strategy designed to protect minority voices through anonymity—actually reduced psychological safety and satisfaction despite increasing participation, while AC improved satisfaction and reduced marginalization without the same psychological costs. The paper frames this through three provocations: (1) anonymity, authenticity, agency, and accountability are distinct constructs that trade off against each other; (2) power asymmetry can reverse the intended effects of protective mechanisms; (3) AI should facilitate group reflection rather than substitute for human responsibility. The paper's structural framework draws on AIMC (AI-Mediated Communication) literature, psychological safety theory, and authenticity as a distinct construct from anonymity. It illuminates the critical distinction between removing identity markers (anonymity) and preserving genuine voice (authenticity), and it reveals that well-intentioned protective mechanisms can backfire in power-imbalanced contexts. What the paper's own framing leaves implicit: it does not structurally explain WHY anonymity reduces psychological safety—it describes the phenomenon but lacks a structural account of the mechanism. It treats the four constructs (anonymity, authenticity, agency, accountability) as a flat set of trade-offs rather than identifying any structural hierarchy or dependency among them. It also does not address what happens to the minority member's relationship to their own position when AI generates autonomous counterarguments on their behalf—the paper notes satisfaction increases but does not examine whether this represents genuine empowerment or a subtler form of displacement.

### Native Reading

The paper presents a multi-phase hybrid intelligence framework for disaster response that integrates crowdsourced data with AI predictions to improve inclusiveness and compassion in relief resource allocation. Its structural framework is a sequential pipeline: (1) extract immediate needs from social media via NLP, (2) predict broader population requirements using AI trained on area vulnerability features, (3) estimate suffering degree via deep learning on satellite imagery to calibrate compassion, and (4) synthesize crowd and AI solutions through a group decision-making method with consensus-building via maximum consensus sequences, all under a stochastic optimization model to handle uncertainty. The paper's core contribution is addressing selective bias—the systematic exclusion of vulnerable populations who lack social media access—by supplementing crowd intelligence with AI-predicted needs. The framework is validated through a case study of the 2021 Henan flood.

What the paper's own framing illuminates well: the structural problem of selective bias in crowdsourced disaster data is clearly identified and the complementarity of crowd intelligence (real-time, granular, but biased) and AI intelligence (broader, predictive, but less contextual) is well-articulated. The consensus-building mechanism for integrating heterogeneous decision-maker inputs is formally specified. What the framing misses or leaves implicit: (a) 'compassion' is operationalized purely as a suffering-degree score derived from satellite imagery—it is treated as a data input rather than an orientational stance, collapsing the motivational dimension into a computational metric; (b) 'inclusiveness' is treated as a coverage problem (reaching more people) rather than a structural property of the decision-making architecture itself; (c) the relationship between the four phases is treated as sequential and additive rather than examined for structural tensions or trade-offs between them; (d) the consensus-building mechanism optimizes for agreement but does not examine whether consensus itself might suppress important minority signals—the very bias problem the framework aims to solve could re-emerge at the decision-integration layer.

### Native Reading

The paper examines how gendered communication inequality shapes food decision-making in low-income households in Banjar Regency, South Kalimantan, using Muted Group Theory (MGT) as its analytical lens. Its central claim is that unequal food decisions are not merely responses to economic constraint but are shaped by communication practices embedded in gender-based power relations. The study uses qualitative multiple-case design with in-depth interviews, observation, and documentation analyzed through NVivo 12 Pro. MGT provides the structural framework: dominant groups (male breadwinners) generate the communicative norms of society, and subordinate groups (women) must accommodate those norms to participate, resulting in systematic muting of women's nutritional knowledge and preferences. The paper identifies four key patterns: domination (male control of financial resources translating to food authority), subordination (women's deference to male preferences despite possessing superior nutritional knowledge), acceptance (women internalizing the legitimacy of male decision authority through breadwinner norms), and negotiation (limited, indirect strategies women use to influence food choices within the constraints). The findings illuminate how economic legitimacy (earning income) is conflated with epistemic authority (knowing what food is best), and how communicative accommodation—women adjusting their speech, timing, and framing to male norms—functions as the mechanism through which muting operates in daily practice.

What the paper's own framing illuminates well: the micro-level communicative mechanisms through which structural inequality reproduces itself in domestic food practices; the distinction between economic constraint and communicative constraint as independent contributors to poor food outcomes; the way 'acceptance' functions not as passive resignation but as an active communicative strategy shaped by cultural norms. What the framing misses or leaves implicit: MGT identifies that muting occurs but does not structurally differentiate between types of muting (is silence from fear structurally different from silence from internalized norm acceptance?); the paper treats 'negotiation' as a single category but does not examine whether different negotiation strategies operate at structurally different levels of agency; the relationship between the women's retained nutritional knowledge and their communicative suppression is noted but not structurally theorized—the paper does not ask what happens to knowledge that is possessed but systematically prevented from entering decision-making, treating it as simply 'marginalized' rather than examining whether it reorganizes into alternative structures; and the paper's intervention recommendations (gender-equitable communication programs) do not engage with why breadwinner norms are so structurally resilient despite being nutritionally counterproductive.
