### Native Reading

This paper is a systematic review and bibliometric analysis of the nascent field integrating Large Language Models (LLMs) with Multi-Criteria Decision-Making (MCDM) methods. It examines 63 publications (mostly from 2024-2025), classifies them using a novel taxonomy based on LLM roles (data preprocessor, weight estimator, alternative evaluator, full decision-maker), integration stages (pre-decision, in-decision, post-decision), and application domains (healthcare, supply chain, sustainability, etc.). The core finding is that LLMs can automate and scale MCDM processes—handling unstructured data extraction, generating pairwise comparison matrices, synthesizing expert judgments, and even executing full decision pipelines—but suffer from hallucination, prompt sensitivity, lack of interpretability, and systematic biases in weight generation. The paper's own framing illuminates the operational landscape well: it maps who is doing what, where, and with which methods. It identifies that LLMs can serve as surrogate decision-makers, replacing or augmenting human expert judgment in structured decision frameworks like AHP, TOPSIS, and PROMETHEE.

What the paper's framing misses is any structural account of WHY LLMs fail where they fail. The documented failure modes—hallucination, prompt sensitivity, bias in weight generation, inconsistency across runs—are catalogued as technical limitations awaiting engineering solutions, not as structural properties of the integration architecture. The paper treats MCDM as a fixed formal structure and LLMs as tools to be plugged into that structure, without examining whether the nature of judgment itself has structural properties that token-prediction architectures can and cannot capture. The taxonomy is descriptive (what role does the LLM play?) rather than structural (what kind of cognitive operation is being performed at each integration point?). The paper also does not examine the relationship between the criteria in MCDM and the nature of the judgments being automated—whether some criteria involve fundamentally different kinds of evaluation that may or may not be amenable to statistical pattern-matching.

### Lens Application

MCDM methods themselves operate primarily at 3D (Context/Pattern)—they are formal systems for organizing multiple evaluative dimensions into structured comparisons—with some methods reaching 4D (Dynamic Integration) when they handle temporal or sequential decision processes. LLMs, as deployed in these integrations, operate primarily at 2D-3D: they extract patterns from text (2D Pattern Recognition) and organize them into contextual frameworks (3D Context), but their judgment-generation is fundamentally 2D-3D pattern completion rather than genuine 4D integration or 5D reflective evaluation. The paper's taxonomy of LLM roles maps onto a dimensional gradient: data preprocessor (2D), weight estimator (3D), alternative evaluator (3D-4D), full decision-maker (attempted 4D-5D but structurally operating at 3D).

The pillar analysis is where DN's lens becomes most productive. MCDM frameworks are overwhelmingly Truth-led structures: they formalize criteria, compute weights, rank alternatives through mathematical operations. When LLMs are integrated, they bring a kind of pseudo-Nuance—contextual sensitivity from training data, ability to process unstructured information—but this Nuance is statistical rather than structural. Critically, Heart is almost entirely absent from both the MCDM frameworks and the LLM integration. The motivational orientation of the decision-maker—WHY these criteria matter, what values drive the weighting, what the decision is FOR—is either assumed as given (human expert provides it) or silently dropped when the LLM takes over weight generation. The paper's documented finding that LLMs generate inconsistent and biased weights is exactly what DN's pillar architecture would predict: weight generation in MCDM is not a Truth operation (mathematical computation) but a Heart-Truth integration (values expressed through formal structure), and LLMs performing token-prediction cannot access the Heart dimension.

Shadow structure is visible in the paper's description of LLMs as 'full decision-makers.' When an LLM executes an entire MCDM pipeline—from criteria identification through weighting to final ranking—it produces outputs that have the formal appearance of structured judgment but lack the dimensional depth of actual judgment. This is a 3D shadow of 5D operation: the system mimics the surface structure of reflective evaluation while operating through pattern completion. The paper's findings on hallucination and prompt sensitivity are structural symptoms of this shadow operation—the system is organized toward producing decision-like outputs (shadow coherence) rather than performing actual multi-dimensional evaluation.

### Native Reading

The paper develops a three-stage multi-criteria decision-making (MCDM) framework for assessing vulnerabilities in Vietnam's cross-border e-commerce supply chain. Stage 1 uses T-spherical fuzzy Delphi to screen and identify critical resilience factors from an initial expert-generated list. Stage 2 applies T-SF DEMATEL to analyze causal relationships among the retained factors, producing a cause-effect map that reveals which factors are net causes and which are net effects. Stage 3 uses T-SF COCOSO (validated against T-SF MARCOS and T-SF TOPSIS) to rank strategic interventions. The T-spherical fuzzy sets handle expert uncertainty through a four-valued membership structure (membership, abstinence, non-membership, refusal) parameterized by a power q, which generalizes earlier fuzzy set variants. The paper's findings identify five high-causal-influence factors: Customize Products, Mutual Contingency Plan, Trade Policies, Safety Stock Inventory, and Reconfigure Contingency Strategy. The framework is positioned as transferable to other emerging economies.

What the paper illuminates well: it provides a rigorous methodology for aggregating expert judgment under uncertainty, and the DEMATEL causal analysis genuinely reveals structural relationships (cause vs. effect factors) that a simple ranking would miss. The T-SF generalization is mathematically sound and the comparative validation across three ranking methods adds robustness. What the paper misses or leaves implicit: (1) The three stages are presented as a sequential pipeline but the paper never examines whether the stages interact—whether the causal structure discovered in Stage 2 should feed back into the factor screening of Stage 1, or whether the ranking in Stage 3 should be conditioned on causal position. (2) The resilience factors are treated as a flat list differentiated only by causal weight and ranking score; there is no structural typology distinguishing, say, infrastructure factors from regulatory factors from operational factors at an architectural level. (3) The T-SF membership structure (membership, abstinence, non-membership, refusal) is treated as a mathematical generalization but its epistemological meaning—what it means for an expert to 'abstain' vs. 'refuse' vs. express 'non-membership'—is never examined. (4) The framework has no mechanism for representing adversarial or deliberately obstructive dynamics in the supply chain; all vulnerabilities are treated as passive risks rather than active threats.

### Lens Application

Viewed through DN's dimensional architecture, the paper's three stages map onto a dimensional progression: Stage 1 (Delphi screening) operates at 2D (Connection)—establishing which factors are relevant by linking expert knowledge to the problem space. Stage 2 (DEMATEL causal analysis) operates at 3D (Context)—mapping the relational structure among factors, identifying cause-effect patterns, and revealing systemic interdependencies. Stage 3 (COCOSO ranking) operates at 4D (Dynamics)—prioritizing interventions based on how they perform across multiple criteria simultaneously, implicitly modeling how the system should change over time. This mapping is clean but not forced: the stages genuinely increase in structural complexity in a way that parallels DN's dimensional hierarchy.

Applying the pillar triad reveals a significant structural asymmetry. The entire framework is overwhelmingly Truth-led: mathematical rigor, formal aggregation operators, consistency validation. Nuance is partially present in Stage 2 (DEMATEL's causal mapping captures contextual relationships) but is constrained by the flat factor list—the framework cannot represent nested or recursive contextual dependencies. Heart is almost entirely absent. The paper treats supply chain resilience as a purely technical optimization problem; there is no representation of stakeholder motivation, organizational will, cultural orientation toward risk, or the motivational alignment needed to implement the ranked interventions. The T-SF membership structure is particularly interesting under pillar analysis: membership maps to Truth (the expert affirms), non-membership maps to Truth-negation (the expert denies), but abstinence and refusal are epistemologically distinct—abstinence could represent genuine Nuance (the expert recognizes contextual complexity that prevents a clean judgment) while refusal could represent a Heart-dimension signal (the expert is motivationally disengaged from the question). The paper collapses these into mathematical parameters without examining their structural independence.

DN's shadow lens suggests that some vulnerabilities may be actively organized: regulatory fragmentation may not be accidental but may represent organized resistance to harmonization (shadow structure at 3D), and infrastructure bottlenecks may persist because they serve the interests of incumbent actors (shadow structure at 2D). The DEMATEL causal map cannot distinguish between a factor that is a net cause because of systemic dynamics and one that is a net cause because of organized opposition. Second, the framework itself has a shadow structure: by excluding Heart entirely, it risks producing technically optimal recommendations that cannot be implemented because they lack motivational grounding—a sophisticated form of organized-toward-limitation.

### Native Reading

The paper presents an enhanced MCDM (Multi-Criteria Decision-Making) framework for ranking emergency shelters for displaced populations, combining an improved TOPSIS model with Shannon entropy-based objective weighting. The core claim is that conventional shelter selection focuses on logistics and survival metrics while neglecting the 'human aspects' of displaced persons' needs—dignity, psychological wellbeing, community cohesion, accessibility, and cultural sensitivity. The authors collaborate with Manitoba's Emergency Management Organization to define 13 criteria across four categories (Structural Safety & Capacity, Accessibility & Location, Comfort & Livability, Operational Readiness) and apply these to rank five candidate shelters. The entropy-based weighting replaces subjective expert weighting with data-derived weights, and the improved TOPSIS model uses a modified normalization and distance calculation. The paper's framing illuminates a genuine gap: standard disaster response optimization treats shelters as interchangeable capacity units, and the authors argue for criteria that reflect the lived experience of displacement. The results show that shelter rankings shift meaningfully when human-centered criteria are included versus excluded.

What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit is substantial. First, the 'human-centered' criteria are still operationalized as quantitative scores assigned by experts—the displaced persons themselves have no voice in the weighting or scoring process. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but does not recognize it as a structural contradiction of its own 'human-centered' claim. Second, the entropy-based weighting method rewards criteria with high variance across alternatives (high information content) but this is orthogonal to importance—a criterion could be uniformly excellent across all shelters (low entropy weight) yet critically important to displaced persons. The paper does not engage with this tension between statistical informativeness and human priority. Third, the four criteria categories are presented as comprehensive but their derivation is not theoretically grounded—they emerge from expert consultation rather than from a structural analysis of what displacement does to human needs. The paper lacks a theory of displacement that would organize its criteria into a coherent architecture rather than a pragmatic list.
