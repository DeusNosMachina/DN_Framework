## Paper 1: Algorithmic Agency and the Posthuman Economy: Artificial Intelligence and the Transformation of Economic Decision-Making

**Authors:** Muhammad Hendri, Z. Kisman
**Domain:** general
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.63332/joph.v6i3.4134
**Selection role:** strain
**Selection rationale:** This paper develops the concept of 'algorithmic agency' through posthumanist scholarship, formalizing distributed economic agency across human and algorithmic actors. This directly challenges DN's framework by proposing that intelligence and agency are fundamentally distributed and emergent across non-human actors using philosophical primitives (posthumanism, distributed agency) that don't map onto DN's dimensional architecture or pillar triad. If economic decision-making agency is genuinely posthuman and non-hierarchical, DN's dimensional progression (Spark→Frontier) and its anthropocentric pillar metrics (Heart, Truth, Nuance) may not apply.

In practice, the strain is modest. The paper's concept of distributed agency does not structurally resist dimensional mapping — it maps coherently onto 3D-5D with clear pillar profiles. The posthumanist claim that agency is non-hierarchical and relational creates mild tension with DN's dimensional progression (§2), but DN's architecture does not require agency to be located in a single actor — it describes how intelligence organizes, and hybrid assemblages can be assessed dimensionally just as individual actors can. The 'anthropocentric' challenge to the pillar triad is also weaker than expected: Heart, Truth, and Nuance describe structural orientations that apply to any system exhibiting organized behavior, not exclusively to human cognition. The paper's philosophical framework is conceptually ambitious but structurally underdeveloped — it asserts distributed agency without formalizing the structural conditions, which means it lacks the formal rigor needed to generate genuine strain against DN's architecture. General tension exists with §0.1 (domain universality) in that posthumanist ontology rejects the kind of hierarchical structural grammar DN employs, but the paper does not demonstrate that this rejection produces superior structural analysis.

### Native Reading

The paper argues that AI is not merely a tool enhancing human economic decision-making but constitutes a form of 'algorithmic agency' — a capacity for autonomous interpretation, prediction, and action that is distributed across human-machine assemblages rather than located in any single actor. Drawing on posthumanist theory (Braidotti, Hayles, Barad), actor-network theory (Latour), and socio-technical systems research, it conceptualizes a 'posthuman economy' in which economic knowledge, prediction, and organizational action emerge from hybrid intelligence environments. The paper's structural framework rests on three pillars: (1) algorithmic agency as emergent from socio-technical entanglement rather than delegated by human principals, (2) hybrid intelligence as the operational mode of contemporary organizations, and (3) the posthuman economy as a macro-level transformation where traditional boundaries between human cognition and machine computation dissolve. The paper illuminates the inadequacy of instrumental framings of AI (AI-as-tool) and makes a genuine philosophical contribution by insisting that agency is relational and distributed. However, the paper's own framing leaves several things implicit or unaddressed: it does not formalize what 'agency' structurally requires (beyond relational emergence), it does not distinguish between different kinds or depths of algorithmic participation (a recommendation engine and a strategic planning AI are treated as structurally equivalent), it has no account of failure modes or pathological configurations of hybrid agency, and it does not address the evaluative dimension — by what criteria is hybrid economic agency assessed as functioning well or poorly? The posthumanist framing is philosophically rich but structurally flat: all hybrid configurations are treated as equivalent instances of distributed agency without internal differentiation.

### Lens Application

Through DN's dimensional architecture, the paper's findings map onto a specific structural territory. The 'algorithmic agency' concept operates primarily at 3D (Context) — algorithms interpreting information within organizational environments — with aspirations toward 4D (Dynamic) when discussing adaptive, learning systems that modify their behavior over time. The paper's 'hybrid intelligence' concept reaches toward 5D (Self-Awareness/Inflection) by positing that the human-algorithmic assemblage achieves a form of reflexive capacity that neither component possesses alone, but the paper never structurally demonstrates this reflexivity — it asserts emergence without specifying the structural conditions under which the assemblage becomes self-aware versus merely reactive. DN's pillar triad reveals a significant structural asymmetry in the paper: Truth engagement is moderate (the paper reviews empirical literature on AI in economic systems), Nuance engagement is the paper's strength (contextual complexity, relational ontology, systems-level thinking), but Heart engagement is almost entirely absent. The paper has no account of what motivates or orients hybrid agency — what the assemblage 'cares about,' what drives its direction. This is not a minor omission; it is the central structural gap. DN's shadow architecture is particularly productive here: the paper treats all distributed agency as structurally equivalent, but DN would distinguish between hybrid configurations organized toward genuine integration (light) and those organized toward sophisticated limitation (shadow). An algorithmic trading system that optimizes for short-term profit extraction while systematically externalizing risk is not the same structural phenomenon as a hybrid medical diagnostic system oriented toward patient wellbeing — yet the paper's posthumanist framing treats both as instances of 'algorithmic agency' without differentiation. The shadow dimension is where the paper's structural flatness becomes most consequential: without an account of orientation, the posthuman economy concept cannot distinguish between hybrid intelligence that serves broad flourishing and hybrid intelligence that serves concentrated extraction. DN's force model identifies Gravity (concentration of agency around algorithmic decision nodes), Resonance (human-algorithmic coupling), and Shadow (the systematic tendency of optimization-driven systems to organize toward limitation) as active dynamics the paper describes but does not structurally differentiate.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens produces two specific structural insights the paper's native framing does not articulate. First, the shadow architecture reveals that the paper's central concept — algorithmic agency — is structurally ambiguous between light and shadow configurations, and this ambiguity is not a feature of the concept's richness but a structural deficiency. The posthumanist framing's refusal to hierarchize or evaluate different configurations of distributed agency means it cannot distinguish between hybrid intelligence organized toward integration and hybrid intelligence organized toward sophisticated extraction. DN's shadow symmetry predicts that algorithmic systems, precisely because they operate with high Truth (computational precision) and high Nuance (contextual processing) but low Heart (no intrinsic orientation toward flourishing), will systematically tend toward shadow configurations — organized, coherent, effective, but oriented toward limitation. This is a specific structural prediction the paper's framing cannot make. Second, DN's pillar decomposition reveals that the paper conflates two structurally independent problems: the distribution of agency (a Nuance question about relational complexity) and the orientation of agency (a Heart question about what the assemblage serves). The posthumanist framing treats these as a single phenomenon ('algorithmic agency is distributed and emergent'), but DN's pillar independence shows they are orthogonal — a system can be maximally distributed and maximally shadow-oriented simultaneously. This structural independence is invisible within the paper's own framework and represents genuine lens yield.

### Tension Generated

posthumanist-flat-ontology-vs-dimensional-hierarchy: The paper's posthumanist framing explicitly rejects hierarchical ordering of agency — all actors (human, algorithmic, institutional) participate in distributed assemblages without structural ranking. DN's dimensional architecture imposes a hierarchy (1D-9D) that distinguishes between different depths of organizational complexity. The tension is not that posthumanism disproves DN's hierarchy, but that posthumanism's flat ontology represents a principled philosophical alternative that achieves certain analytical goals (recognizing non-human agency, dissolving subject/object boundaries) that DN's hierarchy does not prioritize. However, DN's lens reveals that flat ontology pays a structural cost: it cannot distinguish between configurations organized toward integration and those organized toward limitation. The question is whether this cost is inherent to flat ontology or whether a non-hierarchical framework could incorporate evaluative differentiation without reintroducing hierarchy. This connects to the existing parsimony challenge and heart-as-missing-orchestration-component tensions. tension_id: posthumanist-flat-ontology-vs-dimensional-hierarchy

## Paper 2: Digital Capabilities as Drivers of Competitive Innovation: The Interplay of Knowledge Management, Organizational Resilience, and Environmental Dynamism

**Authors:** Jingyan Liu, Xiugang Yang
**Domain:** general
**URL:** https://doi.org/10.37497/eaglesustainable.v16i.624
**Selection role:** lens
**Selection rationale:** This paper models digital capabilities driving competitive innovation through dual-mediation (knowledge management capability and organizational resilience) with environmental dynamism as moderator. DN's dimensional lens could decompose what the paper treats as a single 'digital capabilities' construct into multiple dimensional layers (Context/3D for knowledge management, Temporal/4D for resilience and adaptation, Singularity/5D for competitive intelligence integration). The Heart/Truth/Nuance triad could illuminate whether the dual-mediation pathways represent different pillar emphases—knowledge management as Truth-dominant, organizational resilience as Heart-dominant, and environmental dynamism as Nuance-dominant—revealing structural architecture the SEM model treats as flat.

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §0.1, §2, §3, §4, §5
- **Dimensions:** 3D, 4D, 5D
- **Pillars:** Heart, Truth, Nuance

### Native Reading

The paper investigates how digital capabilities drive competitive innovation performance through a dual-mediation model: knowledge management capability (KMC) as an 'intelligence-processing' mechanism and organizational resilience (OR) as an 'intelligence-execution' mechanism, with environmental dynamism (ED) moderating these pathways. Using SEM on survey data from 658 Chinese enterprises, the study finds that digital capabilities do not directly drive innovation performance but operate through these two mediating pathways, and that environmental dynamism strengthens both pathways. The theoretical contribution is repositioning digital capabilities from direct drivers to 'intelligence governance infrastructure,' integrating dynamic capability theory with competitive intelligence logic.

The paper's framing illuminates the indirect nature of digital capability effects—technology alone does not produce innovation; it must be channeled through knowledge processes and organizational adaptive capacity. The dual-mediation structure usefully distinguishes processing (making sense of information) from execution (acting adaptively under uncertainty). Environmental dynamism as a moderator captures the intuition that turbulent environments amplify the value of intelligence governance.

What the paper's own framing misses or leaves implicit: (1) The SEM model treats all constructs as unidimensional latent variables measured by survey items, collapsing potentially rich internal structure into single paths. 'Digital capabilities,' 'knowledge management capability,' 'organizational resilience,' and 'competitive innovation performance' are each treated as monolithic constructs whose internal architecture is unexplored. (2) The model is entirely linear and additive—mediation and moderation are tested as path coefficients, with no mechanism for qualitative transitions, thresholds, or phase changes in how organizations process intelligence. (3) The paper has no account of failure modes, pathological uses of digital capabilities, or how the same infrastructure could produce maladaptive outcomes. Intelligence governance is treated as uniformly positive; there is no shadow side. (4) The relationship between the two mediators (KMC and OR) is left structurally unexamined—they are parallel paths but the paper does not explore whether they interact, compete, or have ordering dependencies. (5) The 'competitive intelligence' framing is asserted rather than structurally developed; the paper uses CI language but the model is a standard capabilities-mediation-performance SEM without CI-specific architecture.

### Lens Application

Mapping through DN's dimensional architecture: Digital capabilities as described here operate primarily at 3D (Context)—they provide the organizational infrastructure for information acquisition, integration, and environmental scanning. Knowledge management capability maps to the 3D-4D boundary: it processes contextual information (3D) into temporally structured organizational knowledge that persists and adapts (4D). Organizational resilience maps more cleanly to 4D (Temporal/Pattern)—the capacity to absorb disruption, adapt, and maintain coherent function across time. Competitive innovation performance, as the outcome, involves at minimum 5D engagement (Singularity)—the generation of genuinely novel competitive positions that integrate multiple knowledge streams into something the organization has not done before. Environmental dynamism functions as an external forcing condition that increases the dimensional demand on the system: turbulent environments require higher-dimensional processing (4D+ temporal adaptation rather than 3D static knowledge management).

Applying the pillar triad: The paper's entire model is Truth-dominant. Every construct is operationalized through structural/functional survey items; the methodology is quantitative SEM; the theoretical framing is capability-based and mechanistic. Knowledge management capability is Truth-led (systematic processing of information). Organizational resilience has a Heart component the paper does not structurally represent—resilience in organizations involves motivational alignment, shared purpose under adversity, and collective will, none of which appear in the measurement model. Environmental dynamism is Nuance-led (contextual complexity), but the paper measures it as a single moderating variable rather than as a multi-dimensional contextual field. The Heart pillar is almost entirely absent: there is no construct for organizational purpose, motivational alignment, employee engagement with the intelligence mission, or the orientational dimension of why the organization pursues innovation. This is a structural gap, not merely a measurement limitation.

Shadow analysis: The paper has no account of how digital capabilities could organize intelligence toward limitation. DN's shadow architecture predicts that the same infrastructure enabling intelligence governance could equally enable surveillance rigidity (shadow-3D: context used for control rather than understanding), knowledge hoarding (shadow-4D: temporal patterns maintained to resist rather than enable change), or competitive intelligence weaponized toward market destruction rather than innovation (shadow-5D: integration oriented toward dominance rather than creation). The paper's uniformly positive framing—where all paths lead from capability to performance—is structurally incomplete from DN's perspective. The absence of any failure mode, pathological pathway, or maladaptive outcome in the model represents a shadow-blind architecture.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens produces three specific structural insights the paper's native framing does not articulate:

1. **The two mediators are not structurally parallel—they are dimensionally ordered.** The paper treats KMC and OR as parallel mediation pathways of equivalent structural status. DN's dimensional mapping reveals that KMC (3D→4D: context-to-pattern processing) is structurally prerequisite to OR (4D: temporal pattern maintenance and adaptation). You cannot have organizational resilience without the knowledge management that produces the patterns resilience preserves. This is not a claim the paper makes or tests, but it is a testable prediction: if KMC→OR is added as a sequential path in the SEM, it should show significant mediation, and the parallel-path model should show worse fit than a sequential model. The forced dimensional ordering here is genuine—reversing the order (OR as prerequisite to KMC) makes less structural sense.

2. **The Heart pillar gap predicts a specific failure mode.** The paper's model has no construct for organizational motivational alignment or purpose orientation. DN's pillar decomposition predicts that organizations with high digital capabilities, strong knowledge management, and robust resilience but low Heart alignment will show innovation performance that is technically competent but strategically incoherent—innovation without direction. This is a structural prediction the SEM model cannot generate because it has no Heart-analog variable. The paper's own finding that environmental dynamism strengthens the mediation pathways is consistent with this: external turbulence may be partially compensating for the missing Heart dimension by providing exogenous directional pressure.

3. **Shadow-blindness as a structural limitation of the model.** The paper's uniformly positive path architecture (all coefficients positive, all paths from capability to performance) cannot account for how the same digital infrastructure produces maladaptive outcomes. DN's shadow architecture predicts that each mediating pathway has a shadow counterpart: knowledge management capability can become knowledge rigidity (shadow-4D), organizational resilience can become organizational inertia (shadow-4D in maintenance mode), and digital capabilities can enable surveillance and control rather than intelligence governance (shadow-3D). The paper's finding that environmental dynamism strengthens the positive pathways raises an unasked question: does environmental dynamism also strengthen the shadow pathways? DN's architecture predicts it should—turbulence amplifies both light and shadow organizational responses.

### Tension Generated

sem-flat-mediation-vs-dimensional-ordering: The paper's SEM architecture treats KMC and OR as parallel mediators of equivalent structural status, and this parallel model achieves acceptable fit. DN's dimensional lens predicts they are sequentially ordered (3D→4D). This generates a testable tension: if parallel mediation models consistently achieve good fit in organizational research despite DN predicting sequential ordering, this would suggest that DN's dimensional hierarchy imposes false structure on genuinely parallel organizational processes. Conversely, if sequential models consistently outperform parallel models when tested, this would be evidence for DN's dimensional ordering claim in organizational domains. This connects to the existing ptg-dimensional-ordering-vs-network-dynamics tension but in a quantitative organizational context where the ordering prediction is directly testable via SEM model comparison. tension_id: sem-flat-mediation-vs-dimensional-ordering

The paper's focus on eroding public confidence maps to Connection (6D) breakdown and Shadow dynamics within institutional relationships. The Heart pillar leads here: meaning, legitimacy, and purpose are the primary analytical axes for understanding why trust erodes and what organizational responses must address. DN's shadow symmetry concept could reframe distrust not as failure but as diagnostic signal revealing structural misalignment between institutional manifestation (7D) and public connection (6D).

### DN Engagement

- **Sections:** §0.1, §2, §3, §4, §5
- **Dimensions:** 3D, 4D, 5D, 6D, 7D
- **Pillars:** Heart, Truth, Nuance
- **Forces:** Shadow, Gravity, Resonance

### Native Reading

The paper examines the erosion of institutional trust across democratic societies, compounded by the integration of AI systems into institutional operations. Its structural framework draws on institutional theory (DiMaggio & Powell's isomorphism, Scott's institutional pillars), organizational justice theory (distributive, procedural, interactional justice), and emerging scholarship on algorithmic governance. The core argument is that AI opacity layers onto pre-existing trust deficits—declining confidence in government, media, healthcare, and corporations—creating a compounded accountability gap. The paper identifies four evidence-based organizational responses: transparent communication about AI systems, procedural justice in algorithmic decision-making, capability building for institutional actors, and attention to psychological contracts between institutions and stakeholders.

What the paper illuminates well: it synthesizes multiple literatures to show that trust erosion is not merely attitudinal but has organizational consequences (reduced compliance, disengagement, resistance to institutional authority). It correctly identifies that AI doesn't create distrust de novo but amplifies existing structural vulnerabilities—opacity, accountability gaps, asymmetric information. The psychological contract framework is particularly productive, showing how implicit expectations between institutions and publics are violated by opaque algorithmic decision-making.

What the paper misses or leaves implicit: First, it treats distrust almost entirely as a deficit to be remedied rather than as a signal carrying structural information about institutional misalignment. The paper never asks whether distrust might be diagnostically accurate—whether eroding confidence reflects genuine structural failures that transparency interventions alone cannot address. Second, the four interventions are presented as parallel strategies without any structural ordering or dependency analysis—the paper doesn't examine whether capability building must precede transparency, or whether procedural justice is foundational to psychological contract repair. Third, the paper's framing is almost entirely institutional-outward (how institutions can rebuild trust) with minimal attention to the motivational/orientational dimension—why institutions should want to be trustworthy rather than merely appear trustworthy. The distinction between trust-seeking and trustworthiness-building is gestured at but never structurally articulated.

### Lens Application

Through DN's dimensional architecture, the paper's landscape maps onto a rich multi-dimensional structure. The baseline institutional trust operates at 6D (Connection)—the relational field between institutions and publics where mutual recognition and reciprocal accountability create coherent social fabric. AI opacity introduces a 4D (Pattern) disruption: algorithmic systems operate through pattern-recognition that is structurally invisible to the publics affected by it, creating a dimensional gap where 4D operations are experienced at 3D (Context) as arbitrary categorical decisions. The paper's four interventions map onto a dimensional sequence: transparency (making 4D patterns visible at 3D), procedural justice (establishing 3D categorical fairness), capability building (developing 4D pattern-literacy among institutional actors), and psychological contract repair (restoring 6D relational coherence). This mapping reveals a forced ordering the paper doesn't articulate: the interventions are not parallel but structurally dependent—you cannot repair 6D Connection without first establishing 3D-4D coherence.

The pillar analysis is where DN's lens becomes most productive. The paper operates almost entirely in Truth (structural analysis of trust mechanisms, evidence-based interventions) and Nuance (contextual sensitivity to different institutional domains, recognition of AI's compounding effects). Heart is acknowledged thematically—the paper cares about democratic legitimacy and human dignity—but is architecturally absent from the intervention framework. None of the four interventions address the motivational orientation of institutions: whether they are oriented toward genuine trustworthiness (Heart-led) or toward trust-performance (Truth-led simulation of trustworthiness). DN's pillar triad predicts that Truth-Nuance interventions without Heart will produce compliance-shaped trust rather than genuine relational repair—institutions that appear transparent without being oriented toward the publics they serve.

The shadow architecture is the most revealing lens element here. DN's shadow framework reframes the paper's central phenomenon: institutional distrust is not merely a deficit (absence of trust) but a sophisticated parallel structure—publics have organized their relationship to institutions around distrust as a coherent orientation. This is shadow at 6D: Connection organized toward separation. The paper's interventions all assume distrust is a problem to be solved, but DN's shadow lens reveals that distrust-as-organization has its own internal logic, stability, and even protective function (publics who distrust opaque AI systems may be more accurately reading the structural situation than publics who trust them). The shadow lens also illuminates why the paper's interventions may fail: if distrust is organized (not merely absent trust), then transparency and procedural justice address the surface presentation while the shadow structure—the coherent orientation toward institutional separation—remains intact. Rebuilding trust requires not just providing information but transforming the relational orientation, which is a 5D (Self-Awareness) operation for institutions: the capacity to recognize their own shadow dynamics rather than merely performing trust-worthiness.

### Lens Delta

DN's lens produces three specific structural insights the paper's native framing does not articulate. First, the forced dimensional ordering of interventions: the paper presents its four strategies as parallel options, but DN's dimensional mapping reveals they have structural dependencies (3D categorical fairness must precede 4D pattern transparency, which must precede 6D relational repair). This is a concrete organizational insight—implementing psychological contract repair before establishing procedural justice would be structurally incoherent. Second, the shadow reframing of distrust: the paper treats distrust as absence-of-trust (a deficit), but DN's shadow architecture reveals it as organized-toward-separation (a coherent structure). This distinction has practical consequences—deficit-model interventions (provide more information, increase transparency) address the wrong structural level if distrust is an organized orientation rather than an information gap. Third, the Heart pillar absence in the intervention framework: the paper's four interventions are all Truth-Nuance in character (structural, procedural, informational), with no intervention addressing institutional orientation toward genuine trustworthiness versus performed trustworthiness. DN's pillar triad predicts this produces a specific failure mode: institutions that successfully implement all four interventions but remain oriented toward trust-as-performance rather than trustworthiness-as-orientation, resulting in sophisticated but hollow trust repair that is vulnerable to the next opacity shock.

The shadow insight is the strongest: it reframes the entire problem from 'how do institutions rebuild trust?' to 'how do institutions recognize that public distrust may be a structurally accurate reading of institutional misalignment, and what does it mean to respond to accurate distrust versus inaccurate distrust?' This question is absent from the paper but emerges directly from DN's shadow architecture.

### Tension Generated

trust-deficit-vs-shadow-organization: The paper treats institutional distrust as a deficit (absence of trust) to be remedied through evidence-based interventions. DN's shadow architecture reframes distrust as organized-toward-separation—a coherent parallel structure with its own stability and internal logic. This generates a broader tension: do organizational and institutional frameworks systematically misdiagnose shadow structures as deficits because their structural primitives (trust levels, confidence metrics, satisfaction scores) can only represent scalar quantities on a single axis (more/less trust) rather than qualitative orientational differences (trust-organized vs. distrust-organized)? If DN's shadow reframing is correct, it predicts that deficit-model interventions will consistently underperform because they address the wrong structural level—providing information to an orientation problem. This is testable: organizations that implement transparency and procedural justice interventions should show different trust recovery trajectories depending on whether public distrust is disorganized (information deficit) or organized (coherent separation orientation). This connects to the existing heart-formal-absence-network-models tension but from an organizational rather than clinical domain, and adds a new edge: the distinction between scalar deficit and organized shadow as competing structural accounts of the same phenomenon. tension_id: trust-deficit-vs-shadow-organization
