### Native Reading

The paper is a scholarly reconstruction of the joint and divergent philosophical projects of Mamardashvili and Pyatigorsky, centered on their 1974 'metatheory of consciousness.' Their shared starting point is that consciousness is not reducible to psychological content or logical form but is a 'universal synthesizing category' that combines content and form through symbolic activity. They call this project 'symbology'—the study of how different symbol systems are generated in the process of understanding. The metatheory treats consciousness as a 'sphere of symbols and interpretations' and seeks structural description of consciousness that is prior to any particular discipline (psychology, logic, linguistics). After the joint work, the two thinkers diverge: Mamardashvili moves toward 'real psychology,' grounding consciousness in the individual self and the Western Cartesian tradition—consciousness as always someone's consciousness, with personality as the metaphysical center. Pyatigorsky moves toward 'observational philosophy,' combining phenomenology with Buddhist theory to develop an impersonal account where the 'observer' is itself a structure of consciousness rather than a person, and observation is a transpersonal process.

The paper illuminates the structural ambition of the metatheory: it seeks a universal grammar of consciousness that is not domain-specific, not reducible to content, and not merely formal. It also illuminates the productive tension in the divergence—Mamardashvili's insistence on the irreducibility of the personal/experiential pole versus Pyatigorsky's insistence on the impersonal/structural pole. What the paper's own framing leaves implicit or underdeveloped: (1) it does not formalize the relationship between the 'content/form synthesis' and the 'symbol' beyond narrative description—the metatheory's structural primitives are gestured at but not rigorously specified; (2) it does not address whether the two divergent paths are complementary aspects of a single architecture or genuinely incompatible ontologies; (3) it does not examine what the metatheory's 'universal synthesizing category' actually synthesizes across—what domains, what levels, what dimensions of organization; (4) the paper treats the Mamardashvili/Pyatigorsky divergence as a historical fact rather than analyzing it as a structural problem within the metatheory itself.

### Native Reading
